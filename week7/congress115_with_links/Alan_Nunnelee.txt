'''Patrick Alan Nunnelee''' (October 9, 1958 – February 6, 2015) was an [[United States|American]] politician who was the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|MS|1}} from 2011 until his death in 2015. Previously he served in the [[Mississippi State Senate]], representing the 6th district, from 1995 to 2011. He was a member of the [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican Party]].

==Early life, education, and business career==
Nunnelee was born in [[Tupelo, Mississippi]], on October 9, 1958.<ref name=votesmart>{{cite web|url=http://www.votesmart.org/bio.php?can_id=8326|title=VoteSmart.org|work=Project Vote Smart|accessdate=October 19, 2014}}</ref> Nunnelee graduated from [[Clinton High School (Clinton, Mississippi)|Clinton High School]] in [[Clinton, Mississippi]], in 1976 and then attended [[Mississippi State University]] (MSU), graduating with a bachelor's degree in 1980. While a student at MSU, he lost much of his eyesight to a degenerative eye disease. Although legally blind he continued his studies. His sight was restored after receiving [[Corneal transplantation|cornea transplants]].<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.clarionledger.com/story/news/2015/02/06/alan-nunnelee-mississippi-congressman-dies/22986865/ | title=Alan Nunnelee, Mississippi congressman, dies at 56 | accessdate=February 9, 2015}}</ref>

Nunnelee was employed by American Funeral Assurance Co., eventually becoming vice president of sales and marketing. His father was also employed by the company, rising to president and CEO. In 1996, Nunnelee and his father founded Allied Funeral Associates, Inc. and Allied Funeral Associates Insurance Company and he has served as Vice-President and Director of both entities.<ref name=votesmart/>

Before beginning his political career he was a popular speaker, crediting God, organ donors, and organizations such as the [[Lions Clubs International|Lions Club]] for having his eyesight restored.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://beatricedailysun.com/news/national/gop-rep-nunnelee-of-miss-dies-after-brain-cancer-stroke/article_109258af-d178-55b7-8d20-6e117f880c4a.html | title=BeatriceDailySun | accessdate=February 9, 2015}}</ref>

==Mississippi Senate==

===Elections===
In 1995, incumbent Republican State Senator [[Roger Wicker]] of the 6th district resigned in order to take up a seat in the [[U.S. House of Representatives]]. Nunnelee ran and won. In 1999, he won re-election to a second term unopposed.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=371288|title=Our Campaigns – MS State Senate 06 Race – November 2, 1999|accessdate=October 19, 2014}}</ref> In 2003, he won re-election to a third term with 69% of the vote.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=210764|title=Our Campaigns – MS State Senate 06 Race – November 4, 2003|accessdate=October 19, 2014}}</ref> In 2007, he won re-election to a fourth term with 66% of the vote.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=382152|title=Our Campaigns – MS State Senate 06 Race – November 6, 2007|accessdate=October 19, 2014}}</ref>

==U.S. House of Representatives==

===Elections===

====2010====
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Mississippi, 2010#District 1}}
In the Republican primary for {{ushr|MS|1}}, Wicker's old congressional seat, Nunnelee 52% defeated Henry Ross (33%) and [[Angela McGlowan]] (15%).<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=554960|title=Our Campaigns – MS District 1 – R Primary Race – June 1, 2010|accessdate=October 19, 2014}}</ref> In the November 2010 general election, Nunnelee defeated Democratic incumbent U.S. Congressman [[Travis Childers]] 55%-41%.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=496787|title=Our Campaigns – MS – District 01 Race – November 2, 2010|accessdate=October 19, 2014}}</ref>

====2012====
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Mississippi, 2012#District 1}}
After redistricting, [[Eupora, Mississippi|Eupora]] Mayor Henry Ross and businessman Robert Estes ran against Nunnelee in the Republican primary. Nunnelee won the March 2012 primary with 57% of the vote. Ross received 29% and Estes 14%.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.politico.com/2012-election/map/#/House/2012/Primary/MS|title=2014 Election Results Senate: Live Map by State, Midterm Races  Races|work=POLITICO|accessdate=October 19, 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=751578|title=Our Campaigns – MS District 01 – R Primary Race – March 13, 2012|accessdate=October 19, 2014}}</ref>

====2014====
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Mississippi, 2014#District 1}}
Nunnelee ran for re-election in 2014. He was the only Congressman in Mississippi who did not face a primary opponent in 2014.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://blog.gulflive.com/mississippi-press-news/2014/03/political_buzz_alan_nunnelee_l.html |title=Political buzz: Alan Nunnelee lone Mississippi congressman without a party primary |publisher=The Mississippi Press |date=March 1, 2014 |accessdate=February 18, 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.sos.ms.gov/elections4.aspx |title=Mississippi Secretary of State June 3, 2014 |publisher=Mississippi Secretary of State |accessdate=February 18, 2015}}</ref>

Nunnelee won the general election with 68% of the vote.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.sos.ms.gov/Elections-Voting/Pages/2014-General-Elections.aspx |title=Mississippi General Election 2014|publisher=Mississippi Secretary of State|date=2014-11-04 |accessdate=February 18, 2015}}</ref>

===Tenure===
In 2011, Nunnelee became a co-sponsor of Bill H.R.3261 otherwise known as the [[Stop Online Piracy Act]].<ref name="govtrack">[http://www.govtrack.us/congress/bill.xpd?bill=h112-3261 Bill H.R.3261]; GovTrack.us; accessed February 6, 2015.</ref>

==Personal life==
Nunnelee married Tori Bedells, a native of [[Clinton, Mississippi]], a graduate of [[University of Southern Mississippi]] Nursing School.<ref>{{Cite news|last=Long|first=Robert Lee|title=Alan Nunnelee wants to serve First District|newspaper=Desoto Times Tribune|date=May 26, 2010|url=http://www.desototimes.com/articles/2010/05/26/news/local/doc4bfdb0f82cd99896383477.txt}}</ref> They had three children.

==Death==
Nunnelee underwent brain surgery at [[University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center]] after the discovery of a mass in his brain in May 2014. He was brought to [[TIRR Memorial Hermann Hospital]] and later [[Johns Hopkins Hospital]] to undergo [[chemotherapy]] and [[Radiation therapy|radiation]] and receive therapy to restore his speech and mobility on the left side of his body.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.hattiesburgamerican.com/story/news/local/2014/07/01/nunnelee-chemo/11927805|title=Nunnelee to undergo chemotherapy|publisher=''[[Hattiesburg American]]''|date=July 1, 2014|accessdate=July 31, 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.clarionledger.com/story/news/2014/07/30/rep-nunnelee-plans-return-dc-following-brain-surgery/13392785|title=Rep. Nunnelee plans return to D.C. following brain surgery|publisher=''[[Clarion-Ledger]]''|date=July 30, 2014|accessdate=July 31, 2014}}</ref>

In January 2015, Nunnelee was moved into a [[hospice]] after a new inoperable brain tumor was discovered.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.clarionledger.com/story/news/2014/07/30/rep-nunnelee-plans-return-dc-following-brain-surgery/13392785|title=Mississippi Rep. Alan Nunnelee moved to hospice|publisher=''[[Politico]]''|date=January 30, 2015|accessdate=January 31, 2015}}</ref> He died at his home in Tupelo on February 6, 2015, aged 56.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.clarionledger.com/story/news/2015/02/06/alan-nunnelee-mississippi-congressman-dies/22986865|title=Alan Nunnelee, Mississippi congressman, dies at 56|publisher=''[[Clarion-Ledger]]'' |date=February 6, 2015|accessdate=February 6, 2015}}</ref> President [[Barack Obama]], in an official statement from the [[White House Press Office]], stated: "Michelle and I were saddened to learn of the passing of Representative Alan Nunnelee. Alan represented the people of his beloved Mississippi for two decades, first as a state senator and then in Congress.  A proud son of Tupelo, Alan never wavered in his determination to serve the men and women who placed their trust in him, even as he bravely battled the illness that ultimately took his life. As a Sunday School teacher and a deacon at his church, Alan believed deeply in the power of faith and the strength of American families. Today, our thoughts and prayers are with Alan’s family – his wife Tori, their children and grandchildren, and all those who loved him."<ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/2015/02/06/rep-alan-nunnelee-mississippi-obituary/22737951/ | title=USAToday | accessdate=February 9, 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.whitehouse.gov/the-press-office/2015/02/06/statement-president-passing-representative-alan-nunnelee | title=WhiteHouse.gov | accessdate=February 9, 2015}}</ref>

The funeral services were held on February 9 in [[Calvary Baptist Church (Tupelo, Mississippi)|Calvary Baptist Church]], [[Tupelo, Mississippi]].<ref>{{cite web | url=http://nunnelee.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=444 | title=Nunnelee.house.gov | accessdate=February 9, 2015}}</ref>

'''Jason Thomas Smith''' (born June 16, 1980)  is an [[United States|American]] businessman, politician, and the [[U.S. Representative]] for [[Missouri's 8th congressional district]] after winning a [[Missouri's 8th congressional district special election, 2013|special election]] on June 4, 2013. The district comprises 30 counties, covering just under 20,000 square miles of southeastern and southern Missouri. He is the third-youngest [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]] member of the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. House of Representatives]], behind only [[Elise Stefanik]] of [[New York (state)|New York]] and [[Mike Gallagher (American politician)|Michael Gallagher]] of Wisconsin.

Prior to being elected to Congress, Smith served four terms representing the 150th Legislative District in the [[Missouri House of Representatives]] and one term serving the 120th Legislative District following redistricting in 2012. Smith served as the [[Whip (politics)|Majority Whip]] during the 96th [[Missouri General Assembly]]<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.house.mo.gov/member.aspx?district=150&year=2011|title=Representative Jason Smith|work=mo.gov|accessdate=March 18, 2015}}</ref> and then as the Speaker Pro Tem during the 97th Missouri General Assembly.

== Early life, education, and business career ==
{{BLP sources|date=August 2016}}
Jason Smith was born in [[St. Louis]] to Bill, a [[Pastor|minister]] and [[auto mechanic]], and Mary, a puppy mill owner.

At age 20, Smith earned two degrees from the [[University of Missouri]]; a Bachelor of Science in Agriculture Economics and a Bachelor of Science in Business Administration. While attending law school, Smith served as the Senator representing the law school in the Student Senate, the Vice President of the entire law school, and class secretary. Also while in law school, Smith purchased his family farm to keep it in the family, became a licensed [[Real estate broker|real estate agent]], and formed his own small business specializing in property investment and development. He earned his [[Juris Doctor]]ate in 2004 and returned to Missouri to take the [[Bar examination|bar exam]]. Smith is one of the only members of Congress that is still paying off student loans from college.

After passing the [[The Missouri Bar|Missouri Bar]] in 2004, he returned home to [[Salem, Missouri|Salem]] to practice law at a local law firm in [[Cuba, Missouri]], continue to run his small business and manage his family farm. The farm, located just outside Salem, Missouri, has been in Smith's family for four generations.

Smith is a lifetime member of the [[National Rifle Association]] and an avid hunter<ref>{{cite web |url=http://electjasonsmith.com/smith-leads-effort-to-overturn-ammunition-and-tackle-ban-the-salem-news-online-february-9-2017/}}</ref>. He attends Grace Community Church in Salem, an [[Assemblies of God|Assemblies of God Church]]. He used to be a board member of the Missouri Community Betterment Association, [[Court Appointed Special Advocates]] (CASA), and president of the Salem FFA Association.

== Missouri House of Representatives ==

=== Elections ===
Following the resignation of the incumbent [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic]] State Representative [[Frank Barnitz]], Smith ran for Missouri's 150th Legislative District, which covered portions of [[Dent County, Missouri|Dent]], [[Phelps County, Missouri|Phelps]], [[Crawford County, Missouri|Crawford]], and [[Reynolds County, Missouri|Reynolds]] counties. Smith defeated Democratic challenger Bobby Simpson 54%–44%.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=245691|title=Our Campaigns – MO State House 150 – Special Election Race – Nov 08, 2005|work=ourcampaigns.com|accessdate=March 18, 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.sos.mo.gov/elections/special/110805-rep150.asp|title=SOS, Missouri – Elections: Special Election – November 8, 2005 – District 150, Missouri House of Representatives|work=mo.gov|accessdate=March 18, 2015}}</ref> At the age of 25, Smith just barely made the constitutional requirements to be a state representative and became the youngest member of the [[Missouri House of Representatives]]. During his first year in office, Smith served as Majority Assistant Deputy Whip<ref name="auto">{{Cite web|title = Missouri House of Representatives|url = http://www.house.mo.gov/content.aspx?info=/bills061/member/mem150.htm|website = www.house.mo.gov|accessdate = September 9, 2015}}</ref> and served on the Agriculture Policy Committee, Appropriations—Education Committee, and the Judiciary Committee.<ref name="auto"/>

One year after being elected to the Missouri House of Representatives in a special election, Smith defeated Democrat Jim O'Donnell 64%–32%.  In his first full term, Smith served as the Vice-Chairman of the Special Committee on Job Creation and Economic Development.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Missouri House of Representatives|url = http://www.house.mo.gov/content.aspx?info=/bills071/member/mem150.htm|website = www.house.mo.gov|accessdate = September 9, 2015}}</ref>

In his third election in just three years, Smith received 70% of the vote, defeating Democrat James D. Ellis in 2008 to secure his second full term in the Missouri House of Representatives.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=446406|title=Our Campaigns – MO State House 150 Race – Nov 04, 2008|work=ourcampaigns.com|accessdate=March 18, 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.sos.mo.gov/enrweb/allresults.asp?eid=256|title=State of Missouri - Election Night Results|publisher=Missouri Secretary of State - IT}}</ref>

In November 2010, Smith was unopposed in his reelection campaign to his fourth full term to the Missouri House of Representatives.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.sos.mo.gov/enrweb/raceresults.asp?eid=300&oid=67699&arc=|title=State of Missouri - Election Night Results|publisher=Missouri Secretary of State - IT}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=642224|title=Our Campaigns – MO State House 150 Race – Nov 02, 2010|work=ourcampaigns.com|accessdate=March 18, 2015}}</ref> Following his election, Smith was elected by his peers to serve as one of the youngest Majority Whips to serve in the Missouri House of Representatives.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Missouri House of Representatives|url = http://www.house.mo.gov/houseleadership.aspx?year=2011|website = www.house.mo.gov|accessdate = September 9, 2015}}</ref>

Following his 2010 election, Smith was again unopposed in his final election to the Missouri House of Representatives in 2012.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Our Campaigns - MO State House 120 Race - Nov 06, 2012|url = http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=761954|website = www.ourcampaigns.com|accessdate = September 9, 2015}}</ref> Upon the start of the 97th General Assembly in 2013, Smith was elected by his peers to serve as Speaker Pro-Tem of the Missouri State House of Representatives.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Our Campaigns - MO State House Speaker Pro Tem Race - Jan 09, 2013|url = http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=783260|website = www.ourcampaigns.com|accessdate = September 9, 2015}}</ref>

==U.S. House of Representatives==
{{Main|Missouri's 8th congressional district special election, 2013}}

=== 2013 Special Election ===
Smith ran for the vacant [[Missouri's 8th congressional district|8th congressional district of Missouri]] seat after incumbent Republican [[U.S. Representative]] [[Jo Ann Emerson]] resigned to accept a CEO position with the [[National Rural Electric Cooperative Association]].

Per Missouri statute, Smith was selected by the 8th District Republican Central Committee to be the party's nominee in the June special election. The selection process—which began with 27 candidates and narrowed to 13 on nomination day—lasted six total rounds before Smith was the last one standing as the Republican nominee on February 9, 2013. Some of the other candidates included State Representative [[Todd Richardson]] of [[Poplar Bluff, Missouri|Poplar Bluff]], former State Treasurer of Missouri and U.S. Representative [[Wendell Bailey]], former State Senator [[Jason Crowell]] of [[Cape Girardeau, Missouri|Cape Girardeau]], [[Lieutenant Governor of Missouri|Lieutenant Governor]] [[Peter Kinder]], former State Treasurer of Missouri [[Sarah Steelman]], State Representative [[Clint Tracy]] of [[Cape Girardeau, Missouri|Cape Girardeau]], and State Senator Wayne Wallingford of [[Cape Girardeau, Missouri|Cape Girardeau]].

In the June special election, Smith was challenged by [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic]] State Representative [[Steve Hodges]] of [[East Prairie, Missouri|East Prairie]], businessman Doug Enyart of the [[Constitution Party (United States)|Constitution Party]], and [[Libertarian Party (United States)|Libertarian]] Bill Slantz. He was declared the winner of the special election on June 4, 2013.<ref>{{Cite web|title = SOS, Missouri - Elections: Special Election - June 4, 2013 – U.S. Congress, District 8|url = http://www.sos.mo.gov/elections/special/20130604-usdist8.asp|website = www.sos.mo.gov|accessdate = September 9, 2015}}</ref> The election marked the 47th consecutive U.S. House race in Missouri in which Democrats failed to pick up a Republican-held seat dating back to 1994 – the second longest Democratic pick-up drought in the nation.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://editions.lib.umn.edu/smartpolitics/2013/06/05/missouri-democratic-us-house-p/ |title= Missouri Democratic US House Pick-Up Drought Extends to 47 |work=Smart Politics |first=Eric |last=Ostermeier |date= June 5, 2013}}</ref>

===2017===
[[File:Jason Smith's Sun Tax Comment.webm|thumb|250px|right|Jason Smith's comments on tanning salon taxes]]
On March 8, 2017, Smith made a controversial statement in a committee meeting. During debate about a tanning salon tax under the [[Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act|Affordable Care Act]], he wondered aloud why there was no tax on the Sun since it was the leading cause of skin cancer. He argued the tanning tax was unfair to women since women go to tanning salons more than men.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.deathandtaxesmag.com/321662/jason-smith-obamacare-tanning-beds-tax-sun/|title=GOP rep wonders why Obamacare taxes tanning salons instead of the sun|date=March 8, 2017|work=Death and Taxes|access-date=March 9, 2017}}</ref>

As a member of the House Ways and Means Committee, Smith had a key role in writing and passing the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, the first major rewrite of the U.S. tax code in 31 years.

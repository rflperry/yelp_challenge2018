'''Brendan Francis Boyle''' (born February 6, 1977) is a [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic]] member-elect of the [[U.S. House of Representatives]] for [[Pennsylvania's 13th congressional district]], having won 67% of the vote on November 4, 2014. The district covers parts of [[Philadelphia]] and [[Montgomery County, Pennsylvania|Montgomery County]]. He was a member of the [[Pennsylvania House of Representatives]] from the [[Pennsylvania House of Representatives, District 170|170th District]] since 2009.

==Early life and education==
The oldest son of Francis, an Irish immigrant, and Eileen Boyle, Brendan was born and raised in the Olney neighborhood of [[Philadelphia, Pennsylvania]]. He attended Roman Catholic parochial schools before receiving an academic scholarship to the [[University of Notre Dame]]. He graduated from Notre Dame with a degree in Government. He attended graduate school at [[Harvard University]]'s [[John F. Kennedy School of Government]], where he earned a master's degree in Public Policy.<ref name="biodata">[http://voteboyle.com/about-brendan Brendan Boyle biodata], voteboyle.com; accessed November 9, 2014.</ref>

==Pennsylvania House of Representatives==
===Elections===
Boyle ran unsuccessfully for the Pennsylvania House in 2004 and 2006, losing both times to then 20 year Republican incumbent [[George T. Kenney]] and being outspent by a nearly 10 to 1 margin. On November 4, 2008, Boyle defeated Republican Matthew Taubenberger, son of [[Philadelphia mayoral election, 2007|2007 mayoral candidate]] [[Al Taubenberger]], by a margin of 15,442 (59.2%) to 10,632 (40.8%) to win the election.<ref name="pahouseelectionreturns">[http://www.electionreturns.state.pa.us/ElectionsInformation.aspx?FunctionID=13&ElectionID=28&OfficeID=13#161 Pennsylvania election returns] (2008); accessed November 9, 2014.</ref> He became the first Democrat ever elected to represent the [[Pennsylvania House of Representatives, District 170|170th House district]].<ref name="biodata"/><ref name="Shaheeli"/><ref name="pahouseelectionreturns"/>

On November 2, 2010, Boyle won re-election, defeating Republican Marc Collazzo by a margin of 64% to 36%.<ref>[http://www.electionreturns.state.pa.us/ElectionsInformation.aspx?FunctionID=13&ElectionID=39&OfficeID=13#170 Pennsylvania election returns (2010]; accessed November 9, 2014.</ref><ref name="pahouseelectionreturns"/> 

In 2010, his brother, [[Kevin J. Boyle|Kevin]], was also elected to a seat in the Pennsylvania House of Representatives.<ref>{{cite web|author=Catherine Lucey|title=Kevin Boyle trips Perzel for Pa. House seat|publisher=Philly.com|date=November 3, 2010|url=http://articles.philly.com/2010-11-03/news/24954287_1_perzel-corruption-charges-house-seat}}</ref> Kevin defeated former Speaker of the House [[John M. Perzel]]. Brendan and Kevin Boyle made history as the first brothers to serve together in the Pennsylvania House.<ref>{{cite web|author=Monica Yant Kinney|title=Philadelphia's Brothers Boyle: Outsiders who made it in|publisher=Philly.com|date=November 14, 2010|url=http://articles.philly.com/2010-11-14/news/24955597_1_grad-school-pennsylvania-house-charter-school-proponent|accessdate=November 9, 2014}}</ref>

For the 2012 election cycle, Brendan Boyle was selected as Chairman of the House Democratic Campaign Committee, the campaign arm of the House Democratic Caucus.<ref name="hdcc">{{cite web|author=Keegan Gibson|title=Exclusive: Boyle to Chair HDCC|publisher=PoliticsPA|date=June 21, 2011|url=http://www.politicspa.com/exclusive-boyle-chair-hdcc/25511|accessdate=November 9, 2014}}</ref>

===Tenure===
As a state lawmaker, Boyle's focus has been on greater educational access. During his first term in office, he introduced the REACH Scholarship program, which would offer tuition-free public college for qualifying Pennsylvania students. He fought cuts to public K-12 and higher education funding, and has supported greater investment in infrastructure, voting for a 2013 bill that provided the first comprehensive transportation funding overhaul since the 1990s. He was a founding member of the LGBT Equality Caucus during his first term in office. In 2014, he introduced legislation to amend Pennsylvania's hate crimes statutes to include crimes perpetrated based on sexual orientation.<!-- introduced but did it pass? --><ref name="biodata"/>

==2014 Congressional campaign==
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Pennsylvania, 2014#District 13}}
In April 2013, he announced his candidacy for [[Pennsylvania's 13th congressional district]], a seat being vacated by the incumbent congressperson, [[Allyson Schwartz]], who declined to run for reelection to make an unsuccessful run for Governor. Boyle had the support of nearly 30 labor unions across Philadelphia and its environs.<ref name="Shaheeli">{{cite web|author=Joe Shaheeli|title=Pols on the Street: Brendan Boyle Says He's In!|publisher=The Philadelphia Public Record|date=May 30, 2013|url=http://www.phillyrecord.com/2013/05/pols-on-the-street-brendan-boyle-says-hes-in|accessdate=November 9, 2014}}</ref>

Despite early polling showing a nearly 30 point lead for former Congresswoman [[Marjorie Margolies]], on May 20, 2014 Boyle won the primary with 41% of the vote, in a multi-candidate race. He won 69% of the vote in the City of Philadelphia. He went on to win the seat in the general election on November 4, 2014, defeating Republican Carson Adcock with 67% of the vote.<ref>[http://www.legis.state.pa.us/cfdocs/legis/home/member_information/house_bio.cfm?id=1160 Representative Brendan Boyle profile] Pennsylvania House of Representatives official website; accessed November 9, 2014.</ref><ref>{{CongLinks|ballot=Brendan_Boyle|votesmart=47357|fec=H4PA13199|followthemoney=6805}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Brendan is married to Jennifer, a Montgomery County public school teacher; the couple has one child. Kevin Boyle serves as a representative of Pennsylvania's 172nd House district, making them currently the only brothers serving simultaneously in the Pennsylvania General Assembly. {{cn|date=November 2014}}

In August 2008, Brendan Boyle was named "one of top 10 rising stars" in politics by the ''[[Philadelphia Daily News]]''.<ref>{{cite web|title=Here are 10 under 40 who are moving into position|publisher=Philly.com|date=August 4, 2008|url=http://articles.philly.com/2008-08-04/news/24991032_1_allies-neighborhood-payton}}</ref> In 2011, the Aspen Institute chose Boyle as one of its Rodel Fellows,<ref>{{cite web|title=Aspen Institute-Rodel Fellowship Class of 2011| publisher=The Aspen Institute|url=http://www.aspeninstitute.org/leadership-programs/aspen-institute-rodel-fellowships-public-leadership/rodel-fellows-class-2011|accessdate=November 9, 2014}}</ref> a program that "seeks to enhance our democracy by identifying and bringing together the nation's most promising young political leaders."<ref>{{cite web|title=The Aspen Institute Selects "Rising Stars" in Governance for its Rodel Fellowships in Public Leadership Program|url=http://www.aspeninstitute.org/leadership-programs/aspen-institute-rodel-fellowships-public-le-/about-rodel-fellowship-program|accessdate=November 9, 2014}}</ref>

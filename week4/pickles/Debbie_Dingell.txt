'''Deborah Ann "Debbie" Dingell''' (née '''Insley'''; November 23, 1953) is the wife of Congressman [[John Dingell]] and a figure in the Michigan [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]]. Dingell worked as a consultant to the American Automobile Policy Council.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.crainsdetroit.com/article/20091026/FREE/910269972 |title=Debbie Dingell to take new post at American Automotive Policy Council|publisher=[[Crain Communications]] |work=Crain's Detroit Business |date=October 26, 2009 |last=Beene |first=Ryan |accessdate=March 22, 2013}}</ref> She was a [[superdelegate]] for the 2012 Democratic National Convention in [[Charlotte, North Carolina]].<ref name=wapoakers>{{cite web |url=http://blog.washingtonpost.com/sleuth/2008/02/angstridden_superdelegate_and.html |title=Debbie Dingell: Angst-ridden Superdelegate and Congressional Spouse|publisher=''[[The Washington Post]]'' |work=The Sleuth (blog) |last=Akers |first=Mary Ann |date=February 27, 2008 |accessdate=March 22, 2013}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.people.com/people/archive/article/0,,20093934,00.html|title=Congressman John Dingell Makes Washington Quake, but Not His Executive Wife, Debbie|publisher=People.com|accessdate=25 November 2014}}</ref>

She is active in several Michigan and Washington, D.C., charities and serves on a number of charitable boards.  She serves as Vice Chair of the Barbara Karmanos Cancer Center and is a member of the Executive Committee, where she co-chairs the Breast Cancer Committee and the Government Relations Committee. She is also a member of the Board of Directors for [[Vital Voices Global Partnership]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.vitalvoices.org/about-us/board-directors |title=Board of Directors |publisher=Vital Voices |date= |accessdate=March 23, 2013}}</ref> She is a 1975 graduate of the [[Edmund A. Walsh School of Foreign Service]] at [[Georgetown University]].

Descended from one of the Fisher brothers, owners of [[Fisher Body]], a GM founder,<ref>[http://www.teddydavis.org/article/roll_call/dingells_powerful_wife.html] {{dead link|date=November 2014}}</ref> she has served as vice chairman of the [[General Motors]] Foundation and as executive director of Global Community Relations and Government Relations at GM. She is a member of the Democratic National Committee from Michigan and chaired Vice President [[Al Gore]]’s campaign in [[Michigan]] in 2000. In 2004, she also helped secure the Michigan Democratic primary and general election vote for [[John Kerry]] in [[Michigan]].

In November 2006, Dingell was elected to the Board of Governors of [[Wayne State University]] in Detroit.<ref name=BOG>{{cite web |url=http://bog.wayne.edu/members/dingell.php |title=Debbie Dingell |publisher=[[Wayne State University]] |accessdate=March 22, 2013}}</ref> She is scheduled to remain on the board until the expiration of her term at the end of 2014.<ref name=BOG />

Dingell and U.S. [[United States Senate|Senator]] [[Carl Levin]] ([[Democratic Party (United States)|D]] - [[Michigan|MI]]) were the proponents of moving up [[Michigan Democratic primary, 2008|Michigan's Presidential Primary]] before February 5, to attempt to garner greater political influence for Michigan during the [[Democratic Party (United States) presidential primaries, 2008#Disputed primaries|2008 Democratic Primaries]].<ref name=dingelllevin>{{cite news |url=http://www.nytimes.com/2008/03/19/opinion/19levin.html?_r=0 |title=New Hampshire Cheated, Too |last1=Levin |first1=Carl |authorlink1=Carl Levin |last2=Dingell |first2=Deborah |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=March 19, 2008 |accessdate=March 22, 2013}}</ref> which resulted in Michigan almost losing half its delegates' votes in the [[2008 Democratic National Convention#Seating of delegates from Florida and Michigan|Democratic Convention]].<ref name=waposhear>{{cite web |url=http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2007/12/01/AR2007120100722.html |title=DNC Punishes Michigan For Early Primary Date |last=Shear |first=Michael D. |publisher=''[[The Washington Post]]'' |work=PostPolitics (blog) |date=December 2, 2007 |accessdate=March 22, 2013}}</ref>

She married Michigan Congressman [[John Dingell]] in 1981.<ref name=politicoclick>{{cite web |url=http://www.politico.com/click/focus/debbie_dingell.html |title=Debbie Dingell |publisher=''[[Politico]]'' |work=Click |accessdate=March 22, 2013}}</ref> John Dingell became the [[List of members of the United States Congress by longevity of service|longest-serving member]] of the [[United States House of Representatives]] in June 2013 and continued  {{age in years and days|1955|12|13}} to the end of the 113th Congress.<ref name=cnnbash>{{cite web |url=http://politicalticker.blogs.cnn.com/2013/03/11/debbie-dingell-considering-senate-bid-in-michigan/ |title=Debbie Dingell considering Senate bid in Michigan |last=Bash |first=Dana |authorlink=Dana Bash |publisher=[[CNN]] |work=Political Ticker (blog) |date=March 11, 2013 |accessdate=March 22, 2013}}</ref>

When Carl Levin announced his retirement from the US Congress at the end of his term in 2015, Dingell indicated that she was interested in running for his seat.<ref name=cnnbash /> When former Michigan governor [[Jennifer Granholm]] declined to run for the seat, a ''[[Politico]]'' writer declared Dingell to be one of the front-runners for the Democratic nomination, alongside US Representative [[Gary Peters (Michigan politician)|Gary Peters]].<ref name=politicohohmann>{{cite web |url=http://www.politico.com/story/2013/03/jennifer-granholm-carl-levin-seat-89238.html?hp=l2 |title=Jennifer Granholm: No run for Carl Levin’s seat |last=Hohmann |first=James |date=March 22, 2013 |publisher=''[[Politico]]'' |accessdate=March 23, 2013}}</ref>

Dingell indicated that she planned to run for her husband's congressional seat after he announced his retirement.<ref name=politicoallen>{{cite web |url=http://www.politico.com/playbook/0214/playbook13106.html |title=Politico Playbook for Feb. 25, 2014 |last=Allen |first=Mike |date=February 25, 2014 |publisher=''[[Politico]]'' |accessdate=February 25, 2014}}</ref> On August 5 she won the Democratic primary. On November 4 she won the general election, defeating Republican Terry Bowman.<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.mlive.com/news/ann-arbor/index.ssf/2014/11/democrat_debbie_dingell_defeat.html |title= Debbie Dingell defeats Terry Bowman in 12th District U.S. House race |last1= Allen |first1= Jeremy |date= November 4, 2014 |website= MLive Media Group }}</ref>  When Dingell is sworn in, she will be the first U.S. woman in Congress to succeed her husband while he is still alive.<ref>{{cite web |url= http://blog.lib.umn.edu/cspg/smartpolitics/2014/02/debbie_dingell_eyes_historic_w.php |title= Debbie Dingell Eyes Historic Win in 2014 |last1= Ostermeier |first1= Eric |date= February 26, 2014 |website= Smart Politics}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/elections/2014/11/02/women-candidates-glass-ceiling-election-day/18085305/|title=Women poised to break glass ceiling on Election Day|author=Catalina Camia, USA TODAY|date=2 November 2014|publisher=Usatoday.com|accessdate=25 November 2014}}</ref>

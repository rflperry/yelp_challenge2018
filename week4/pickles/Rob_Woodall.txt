'''William Robert Woodall III'''<ref>[http://www.legistorm.com/memberbio/2722/Rep_Rob_Woodall_GA.html]</ref> (born February 11, 1970)<ref>http://nationaljournal.com/politics/rob-woodall-r--20101025</ref> is an [[United States|American]] politician who has been the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|GA|7}} since 2011. He is a member of the [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican Party]]. Prior to being elected to congress, he was the Chief of Staff to U.S. Congressman [[John Linder]] (R-GA). He worked for Linder from 1994 to 2010.

==Early life, education, and career==
Woodall was born in [[Athens, GA]]. He attended both public and private grade schools, and graduated from [[Marist High School (Georgia)|Marist School]] in 1988. He attended [[Furman University]] followed by law school at the [[University of Georgia]]. While attending law school, he spent summers working in a [[Washington, D.C.]] law firm. He left law school after the summer of 1994 to work for his hometown [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] [[John Linder]]. Woodall later finished law school in 1998.<ref>http://robwoodall.com/index.php/meet-rob/</ref>

==U.S. House of Representatives==

===2010 election===
{{Main|United States House of Representatives elections in Georgia, 2010#District 7}}

He won the Republican primary with about 56% of the vote against Jody Hice.<ref>http://www.forsythnews.com/news/archive/5800/</ref> He faced Democrat Doug Heckman in the 2010 General Election.<ref>http://blogs.ajc.com/georgia_elections_news/2010/08/10/woodall-to-face-heckman-for-7th-district-congressional-seat/?cxntfid=blogs_georgia_elections_news</ref> On November 2, 2010, Woodall defeated Heckman to win the election.<ref>http://www.gwinnettdailypost.com/home/headlines/Woodall_to_take_reins_from_Linder_106588723.html</ref>  

The top donors to Woodall's campaign funds were the Credit Union National Association, the Southern Company, the American Dental Association, and the Vision for Tomorrow Fund.<ref>http://www.votesmart.org/candidate/campaign-finance/122251/rob-woodall</ref>  

Woodall addressed the U.S. House on October 26, 2011, in which he called for reducing regulation on businesses.<ref>http://www.votesmart.org/public-statement/649011/moving-the-american-dream-forward</ref>  

===Key Positions and Votes===
Woodall voted for repealing the [[Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act]] in January, 2011.

In July 2011, he voted for the [[Cut, Cap and Balance Act]] during the [[2011 U.S. debt ceiling crisis]].  

In October 2011, Woodall voted for legislation to restrict how private insurance companies listed on a public insurance exchange may offer abortion coverage.<ref>http://www.votesmart.org/candidate/key-votes/122251/rob-woodall</ref> 

Woodall was one of only six Republicans who opposed legislation that would require all states to honor the concealed weapons permits of other states, arguing that the bill was unnecessary because the Second Amendment already gives Americans the right to bear arms.<ref>http://thehill.com/blogs/floor-action/house/194113-house-approves-concealed-weapons-bill</ref> 

Woodall is also one of only six House Republicans in the [[112th United States Congress|112th Congress]] who have not signed [[Grover Norquist|Grover Norquist's]] "[[Americans for Tax Reform|Taxpayer Protection Pledge]]," stating that "my commitment to the [[Fair Tax]] and a common-sense tax overhaul makes it impossible for me to support the second component of the Pledge, which states that I must 'oppose any net reduction or elimination of deductions and credits, unless matched dollar for dollar by further reducing tax rates.'"<ref>{{cite web |url=http://s3.amazonaws.com/atrfiles/files/files/120111-federalpledgesigners.pdf |title=The Taxpayer Protection Pledge Signers, 112th Congressional List |author= |date= |work= |publisher=Americans for Tax Reform |accessdate=9 December 2011}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://thehill.com/homenews/senate/164317-some-gop-nos-on-pledge-could-complicate-debt-talks |title=Some GOP no's on 'pledge' could complicate debt talks |author=Alexander Bolton|date=2 June 2011 |work=The Hill |publisher= |accessdate=9 December 2011}}</ref>

On July 24, 2013 Woodall voted against Representative Justin Amash's (R-Michigan) amendment to HR 2397 which would have ended the National Security Agency's ability to collect and store data on the phone calls of every American without a warrant.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2013/roll412.xml |title=FINAL VOTE RESULTS FOR ROLL CALL 412|author= |date= |work=|accessdate=25 July 2013}} </ref>

===Legislation introduced===
Woodall introduced the [[Baseline Reform Act of 2013 (H.R. 1871; 113th Congress)]] into the House on May 8, 2013.<ref name=1871sum>{{cite web|title=H.R. 1871 - Summary|url=http://beta.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/house-bill/1871|publisher=United States Congress|accessdate=28 March 2014}}</ref> The bill would change the way in which discretionary [[Appropriations bill (United States)|appropriations]] for individual accounts are projected in CBO’s [[Baseline (budgeting)|baseline]].<ref name="cbo1871"/> Under H.R. 1871, projections of such spending would still be based on the current year’s appropriations, but would not be adjusted for [[inflation]] going forward.<ref name=cbo1871>{{cite web|title=CBO - H.R. 1871|url=http://www.cbo.gov/publication/45212|publisher=Congressional Budget Office|accessdate=28 March 2014}}</ref>

===Tenure===
Woodall took office as part of the [[112th United States Congress]] in January 2011. In July 2014, Woodall was elected chairman of the [[Republican Study Committee]], a group of conservative Republican lawmakers, succeeding [[Steve Scalise]].<ref>[http://woodall.house.gov/press-release/woodall-chair-republican-study-committee]</ref>

===Committee assignments===
House Budget Committee<br />
House Rules Committee<br />
House Oversight and Government Reform Committee

'''William Robert Woodall III'''<ref>[http://www.legistorm.com/memberbio/2722/Rep_Rob_Woodall_GA.html]</ref> (born February 11, 1970)<ref>http://nationaljournal.com/politics/rob-woodall-r--20101025</ref> is an [[United States|American]] politician who has been the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|GA|7}} since 2011. He is a member of the [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican Party]]. Prior to being elected to congress, he was the Chief of Staff to U.S. Congressman [[John Linder]] (R-GA). He worked for Linder from 1994 to 2010.

==Early life, education, and career==
Woodall was born in [[Athens, GA]]. He attended both public and private grade schools, and graduated from [[Marist High School (Georgia)|Marist School]] in 1988. He attended [[Furman University]] followed by law school at the [[University of Georgia]]. While attending law school, he spent summers working in a [[Washington, D.C.]] law firm. He left law school after the summer of 1994 to work for his hometown [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] [[John Linder]]. Woodall later finished law school in 1998.<ref>http://robwoodall.com/index.php/meet-rob/</ref>

==U.S. House of Representatives==

===2010 election===
{{Main|United States House of Representatives elections in Georgia, 2010#District 7}}

He won the Republican primary with about 56% of the vote against Jody Hice.<ref>http://www.forsythnews.com/news/archive/5800/</ref> He faced Democrat Doug Heckman in the 2010 General Election.<ref>http://blogs.ajc.com/georgia_elections_news/2010/08/10/woodall-to-face-heckman-for-7th-district-congressional-seat/?cxntfid=blogs_georgia_elections_news</ref> On November 2, 2010, Woodall defeated Heckman to win the election.<ref>http://www.gwinnettdailypost.com/home/headlines/Woodall_to_take_reins_from_Linder_106588723.html</ref>  

The top donors to Woodall's campaign funds were the Credit Union National Association, the Southern Company, the American Dental Association, and the Vision for Tomorrow Fund.<ref>http://www.votesmart.org/candidate/campaign-finance/122251/rob-woodall</ref>  

Woodall addressed the U.S. House on October 26, 2011, in which he called for reducing regulation on businesses.<ref>http://www.votesmart.org/public-statement/649011/moving-the-american-dream-forward</ref>  

===Key Positions and Votes===
Woodall voted for repealing the [[Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act]] in January, 2011.

In July 2011, he voted for the [[Cut, Cap and Balance Act]] during the [[2011 U.S. debt ceiling crisis]].  

In October 2011, Woodall voted for legislation to restrict how private insurance companies listed on a public insurance exchange may offer abortion coverage.<ref>http://www.votesmart.org/candidate/key-votes/122251/rob-woodall</ref> 

Woodall was one of only six Republicans who opposed legislation that would require all states to honor the concealed weapons permits of other states, arguing that the bill was unnecessary because the Second Amendment already gives Americans the right to bear arms.<ref>http://thehill.com/blogs/floor-action/house/194113-house-approves-concealed-weapons-bill</ref> 

Woodall is also one of only six House Republicans in the [[112th United States Congress|112th Congress]] who have not signed [[Grover Norquist|Grover Norquist's]] "[[Americans for Tax Reform|Taxpayer Protection Pledge]]," stating that "my commitment to the [[Fair Tax]] and a common-sense tax overhaul makes it impossible for me to support the second component of the Pledge, which states that I must 'oppose any net reduction or elimination of deductions and credits, unless matched dollar for dollar by further reducing tax rates.'"<ref>{{cite web |url=http://s3.amazonaws.com/atrfiles/files/files/120111-federalpledgesigners.pdf |title=The Taxpayer Protection Pledge Signers, 112th Congressional List |author= |date= |work= |publisher=Americans for Tax Reform |accessdate=9 December 2011}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://thehill.com/homenews/senate/164317-some-gop-nos-on-pledge-could-complicate-debt-talks |title=Some GOP no's on 'pledge' could complicate debt talks |author=Alexander Bolton|date=2 June 2011 |work=The Hill |publisher= |accessdate=9 December 2011}}</ref>

On July 24, 2013 Woodall voted against Representative Justin Amash's (R-Michigan) amendment to HR 2397 which would have ended the National Security Agency's ability to collect and store data on the phone calls of every American without a warrant.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2013/roll412.xml |title=FINAL VOTE RESULTS FOR ROLL CALL 412|author= |date= |work=|accessdate=25 July 2013}} </ref>

===Legislation introduced===
Woodall introduced the [[Baseline Reform Act of 2013 (H.R. 1871; 113th Congress)]] into the House on May 8, 2013.<ref name=1871sum>{{cite web|title=H.R. 1871 - Summary|url=http://beta.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/house-bill/1871|publisher=United States Congress|accessdate=28 March 2014}}</ref> The bill would change the way in which discretionary [[Appropriations bill (United States)|appropriations]] for individual accounts are projected in CBO’s [[Baseline (budgeting)|baseline]].<ref name="cbo1871"/> Under H.R. 1871, projections of such spending would still be based on the current year’s appropriations, but would not be adjusted for [[inflation]] going forward.<ref name=cbo1871>{{cite web|title=CBO - H.R. 1871|url=http://www.cbo.gov/publication/45212|publisher=Congressional Budget Office|accessdate=28 March 2014}}</ref>

===Tenure===
Woodall took office as part of the [[112th United States Congress]] in January 2011. In July 2014, Woodall was elected chairman of the [[Republican Study Committee]], a group of conservative Republican lawmakers, succeeding [[Steve Scalise]].<ref>[http://woodall.house.gov/press-release/woodall-chair-republican-study-committee]</ref>

'''George Kenneth "G. K." Butterfield, Jr.''' (born April 27, 1947) is the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|NC|1}}, elected in 2004. He is a member of the [[United States Democratic Party|Democratic Party]]. The district is located in the northeastern corner of the state, stretching from [[Durham, North Carolina|Durham]] to [[Elizabeth City, North Carolina|Elizabeth City]] and including all or parts of 24 counties.

An attorney, he served as Resident Superior Court judge for North Carolina (1988-2000) and as a state Supreme Court justice, the latter from 2001-2004.

==Early life and education==
Butterfield was born and raised in a prominent [[African-American]] family in [[Wilson, North Carolina]], the son of Addie Lourine (née Davis) and George Kenneth Butterfield, both of mixed race.<ref>[http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~battle/reps/butterfield.htm "Butterfield"]</ref> Butterfield's father immigrated to the United States from [[Bermuda]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.csce.gov/index.cfm?FuseAction=ContentRecords.ViewWitness&ContentRecord_id=984&ContentType=D&ContentRecordType=D&ParentType=H&CFID=27817&CFTOKEN=61678793 |title=CSCE :: Testimony :: Hon. G.K. Butterfield Commissioner - Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe |publisher=Csce.gov |date= |accessdate=2010-08-23}}</ref>

Described by the ''[[Washington Post]]'' as an "African-American who appears to be white,"<ref name="why">[http://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/gk-butterfield-d-nc/gIQAA20hDP_topic.html "G.K. Butterfield, (D-N.C.)"], ''Politics'', ''Washington Post'', 23 December 2011, accessed 4 April 2013</ref> Butterfield acknowledges he has European as well as African ancestry, and that he identifies as African American. He has noted that he grew up on the "black side" of town and led [[civil rights]] marches.<ref>[http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2008/12/14/ap-many-insisting-that-ob_n_150846.html AP, "Many insisting that Obama is not black"], ''Huffington Post'', 14 December 2008, accessed 4 April 2013</ref> He is a member of the Congressional Black Caucus.

Butterfield graduated from Charles H. Darden High School. He earned degrees in [[political science]] and [[sociology]] from [[North Carolina Central University|North Carolina Central University (NCCU)]].<ref name=aboutGK/> In 1974, he received a [[Juris Doctor]] degree from the [[NCCU School of Law]].<ref name=aboutGK/>

Butterfield served in the [[United States Army]] from 1968 to 1970.<ref>[http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=b001251 BUTTERFIELD, George Kenneth, Jr. (G.K.), (1947 - )] Biographical Directory of the United States Congress</ref>

==Judicial career==
In 1988, Butterfield was elected as Resident Superior Court judge in the first judicial division. For the next twelve years, he presided over civil and criminal court in 46 counties of North Carolina.<ref name=aboutGK/> In February 2001, he was appointed to the [[North Carolina Supreme Court]] by Governor [[Mike Easley]].<ref name=aboutGK/> In [[North Carolina judicial elections, 2002|2002]], Butterfield lost his seat on the Supreme Court but returned to the Superior Court bench by special appointment of Governor [[Mike Easley]] and served in that position until his retirement in May 2004.<ref name=aboutGK/>

==Political career==
Butterfield was first elected to the House of Representatives in a special election in 2004, to fill the seat of [[Frank Ballance]].

Butterfield serves on the [[U.S. House Committee on Energy and Commerce|House Committee on Energy and Commerce]], and formerly served on the [[U.S. House Committee on Armed Services|House Armed Services Committee]] and the [[U.S. House Committee on Agriculture|House Agriculture Committee]]. He is the Region VIII representative on the [[Steering and Policy Committee of the United States House of Representatives|House Democratic Steering and Policy Committee]].

Beginning in 2007 in the [[110th Congress]], Butterfield was chosen to serve as one of eight Chief Deputy Whips; they assist in the formulation of Democratic policy and ensure the passage of legislation by maintaining good communication with members. He was appointed to this position by [[Speaker of the United States House of Representatives|Speaker]] [[Nancy Pelosi]] and [[Party whips of the United States House of Representatives|Majority Whip]] [[Jim Clyburn]].<ref name=aboutGK/>

==Political positions==
As a member of the House Committee on Energy and Commerce, G.K. Butterfield advocated for the [[American Clean Energy and Security Act]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thehill.com/special-reports/cap-and-trade-june-2009/8229-poorest-americans-contributing-least-to-climate-change-will-not-be-hurt-by-legislation-to-rectify|title=Poorest Americans, contributing least to climate change, will not be hurt by legislation to rectify|author=Rep. G.K. Butterfield (D-N.C.)|date=06/15/09|publisher=The Hill}}</ref> Butterfield supports "a market-based approach to capping carbon emissions"<ref name="Butterfield">{{cite web|url=http://butterfield.house.gov/index.cfm?sectionid=63&sectiontree=4,63|title=Energy & Global Climate Change|publisher=Congressman G.K. Butterfield Official Website}}</ref> and wants to broaden the United States' sources of energy.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thehill.com/blogs/congress-blog/energy-a-environment/103309-hearing-with-oil-executives-underscores-need-for-energy-overhaul-rep-gk-butterfield|title=Hearing with oil executives underscores need for energy overhaul|date=06/15/10|author=Rep. G.K. Butterfield (D-N.C.)|publisher=The Hill}}</ref> On his website, Butterfield stresses the need to find more clean and domestic sources of energy.<ref name="Butterfield" />

A strong supporter of civil rights, he advocated renewal of the [[Voting Rights Act]] and "introduced a bill calling for the Capitol Visitor's Center to acknowledge the [[Slavery in the United States|slave labor]] used to build the Capitol."<ref name="why"/>

In 2009, Butterfield introduced the ''Don't Let the Bed Bugs Bite Act'' "to assist states in carrying out inspections of lodging facilities, train inspection personnel, contract with a commercial exterminator; educate owners and staff at lodging facilities."<ref name="Afro">http://www.afro.com/sections/news/national/story.htm?storyid=3130</ref> Butterfield also passed H.R. 4252 "To amend the Small Business Act to change the net worth amount under the small business program for socially and economically disadvantaged individuals from $750,000 to $978,722, and for other purposes."<ref name="LOC">http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/query/z?c111:H.R.4253:</ref>
 
Butterfield supported the [[Affordable Health Care Act]], and worked with the Energy and Commerce Committee to help write the legislation.<ref name="Vote-smart">http://www.votesmart.org/speech_detail.php?sc_id=538991&keyword=&phrase=&contain=</ref> During the discussion of the bill in Congress, Butterfield complained about the lack of cooperation from the Republican party.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

Butterfield supports increasing taxes for higher-income families while decreasing taxes for middle and low-income families.<ref name="Vote-smart" />  Although he is an advocate for using government stimulus in order to improve the economy, Butterfield wants to reduce government regulations on the private sector.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

In 2008 [[Planned Parenthood]] gave Butterfield an 80 percent ranking. In 2009 Butterfield supported the interests of [[NARAL Pro-Choice America]] 100 percent of the time.<ref name="Vote-smart">[http://www.votesmart.org/issue_rating_category.php?can_id=41077]</ref>  Butterfield identifies as pro-choice and especially supports legalized abortion when the life of the woman is in danger or if the pregnancy is a result of rape or incest.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

Butterfield has repeatedly voted against defining marriage as being between one man and one woman, voting against the [[Marriage Protection Act]] of 2004 and constitutional marriage amendments in 2004 and 2006. He has voted to ban job discrimination based on sexual orientation, and in 2010 voted for the repeal of [[Don't Ask Don't Tell]].<ref>{{cite web|title=G.K. Butterfield - Gay Marriage|url=http://www.thepoliticalguide.com/Profiles/House/North_Carolina/G.K._Butterfield/Views/Gay_Marriage/|publisher=The Political Guide}}</ref>

Butterfield voted in 2008 against the [[Troubled Asset Relief Program]] (TARP) $700 billion bailout of the financial industry and the $14 billion rescue package for the auto industry.<ref name="WP Butterfield 2012">{{cite web|title=Democrat George Kenneth 'G.K.' Butterfield, Jr.|url=http://www.washingtontimes.com/campaign-2012/candidates/george-kenneth-gk-butterfield-jr-34352/|publisher=Washington Post|accessdate=15 May 2012}}</ref>

In 2011, he voted to extend expiring provisions of the [[PATRIOT Act]]<ref>http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2011/roll036.xml</ref> and voted in favor of the [[National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) for Fiscal Year 2012]].<ref>{{cite web|title=HR 1540 National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2012 - Voting Record|url=http://www.votesmart.org/bill/votes/37467|work=Project Vote Smart|accessdate=5 April 2012}}</ref>

In 2012, Butterfield introduced legislation that, if passed, would require more input from the public before tolls are introduced on roads. His legislation is in response to the "No toll on [[I-95]]" group, which is a Roanoke Rapids-based group that opposes instating a toll on I-95. Opponents of the toll argue that it leads to double taxation, and say it is the first time "the federal government has put tolls on an existing interstate."<ref name="Tolling Bill">{{cite news|title=Butterfield to announce tolling bill|url=http://www.chicagotribune.com/news/sns-mct-butterfield-to-announce-tolling-bill-20120504,0,4387482.story|newspaper=Chicago Tribune|date=4 May 2012}}</ref>

On July 23, 2014, Butterfield introduced the joint resolution [[Approving the location of the National Liberty Monument (H.J.Res. 120; 113th Congress)|Approving the location of a memorial to commemorate the more than 5,000 slaves and free Black persons who fought for independence in the American Revolution]].<ref name=hjres120allactions>{{cite web|title=H.J.Res. 120 - All Actions|url=https://beta.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/house-joint-resolution/120/all-actions|publisher=United States Congress|accessdate=9 September 2014}}</ref> The joint resolution would approve the location of a commemorative work to honor the more than [[Black Patriot (American Revolution)|5,000 slaves and free black persons who fought]] in the [[American Revolution]].<ref name=HillHouseAuthorizes>{{cite news|last1=Marcos|first1=Cristina|title=House authorizes location for American Revolution memorial in D.C.|url=http://thehill.com/blogs/floor-action/house/217018-house-authorizes-location-for-american-revolution-memorial-in-dc|accessdate=9 September 2014|publisher=The Hill|date=8 September 2014}}</ref>

==Political campaigns==

===2004===
Butterfield was elected to Congress in a special election on July 20, 2004 (see above.) to fill the unexpired term of Representative [[Frank Ballance]], who resigned for legal reasons. He defeated [[United States Republican Party|Republican]] candidate Greg Dority and [[United States Libertarian Party|Libertarian Party]] nominee Tom Eisenmenger. Butterfield was sworn into office on July 21, 2004.

On July 20, 2004, Butterfield won the Democratic primary entitling him to run in the November 2004 general election. Running against Dority again, he won his first full term with 64% of the popular vote.<ref>{{cite news|author=William L. Holmes|title=Butterfield Wins Special Election; Will Face Dority in November|date=21 July 2004|publisher=Associated Press|accessdate=March 2, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|author=Cindy George|title=Former Justice Wins 1st District; Butterfield Fills Ballance’s Seat|date=21 July 2004|work=News and Observer|page=A16|accessdate=March 2, 2010}}</ref>

In 1971, Butterfield married [[Jean Farmer-Butterfield|Jean Farmer]]. They have two daughters, Valeisha and Lenai, who are now adults.<ref name=aboutGK/> He and Jean divorced in 1991. Jean is now a member of the North Carolina House of Representatives representing House District 24. Their daughter Valeisha is married to former NBA player, Dahntay L. Jones. They have one child together, Dahntay, Jr. In addition, Butterfield is the father of an adult daughter, Tunya Michelle Butterfield. Tunya and her partner have twin sons, Gavin and Chase.

A strong supporter of civil rights, he advocated renewal of the [[Voting Rights Act]] and "introduced a bill calling for the Capitol Visitor's Center to acknowledge the [[Slavery in the United States|slave labor]] used to build the Capitol."<ref name="why"/>

In 2009, Butterfield introduced the ''Don't Let the Bed Bugs Bite Act'' "to assist states in carrying out inspections of lodging facilities, train inspection personnel, contract with a commercial exterminator; educate owners and staff at lodging facilities."<ref name="Afro">http://www.afro.com/sections/news/national/story.htm?storyid=3130</ref> Butterfield also passed H.R. 4252 "To amend the Small Business Act to change the net worth amount under the small business program for socially and economically disadvantaged individuals from $750,000 to $978,722, and for other purposes."<ref name="LOC">http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/query/z?c111:H.R.4253:</ref>
 
Butterfield supported the [[Affordable Health Care Act]], and worked with the Energy and Commerce Committee to help write the legislation.<ref name="Vote-smart">http://www.votesmart.org/speech_detail.php?sc_id=538991&keyword=&phrase=&contain=</ref> During the discussion of the bill in Congress, Butterfield complained about the lack of cooperation from the Republican party.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

Butterfield supports increasing taxes for higher-income families while decreasing taxes for middle and low-income families.<ref name="Vote-smart" />  Although he is an advocate for using government stimulus in order to improve the economy, Butterfield wants to reduce government regulations on the private sector.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

In 2008 [[Planned Parenthood]] gave Butterfield an 80 percent ranking. In 2009 Butterfield supported the interests of [[NARAL Pro-Choice America]] 100 percent of the time.<ref name="Vote-smart">[http://www.votesmart.org/issue_rating_category.php?can_id=41077]</ref>  Butterfield identifies as pro-choice and especially supports legalized abortion when the life of the woman is in danger or if the pregnancy is a result of rape or incest.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

Butterfield has repeatedly voted against defining marriage as being between one man and one woman, voting against the [[Marriage Protection Act]] of 2004 and constitutional marriage amendments in 2004 and 2006. He has voted to ban job discrimination based on sexual orientation, and in 2010 voted for the repeal of [[Don't Ask Don't Tell]].<ref>{{cite web|title=G.K. Butterfield - Gay Marriage|url=http://www.thepoliticalguide.com/Profiles/House/North_Carolina/G.K._Butterfield/Views/Gay_Marriage/|publisher=The Political Guide}}</ref>

Butterfield voted in 2008 against the [[Troubled Asset Relief Program]] (TARP) $700 billion bailout of the financial industry and the $14 billion rescue package for the auto industry.<ref name="WP Butterfield 2012">{{cite web|title=Democrat George Kenneth 'G.K.' Butterfield, Jr.|url=http://www.washingtontimes.com/campaign-2012/candidates/george-kenneth-gk-butterfield-jr-34352/|publisher=Washington Post|accessdate=15 May 2012}}</ref>

In 2011, he voted to extend expiring provisions of the [[PATRIOT Act]]<ref>http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2011/roll036.xml</ref> and voted in favor of the [[National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) for Fiscal Year 2012]].<ref>{{cite web|title=HR 1540 National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2012 - Voting Record|url=http://www.votesmart.org/bill/votes/37467|work=Project Vote Smart|accessdate=5 April 2012}}</ref>

In 2012, Butterfield introduced legislation that, if passed, would require more input from the public before tolls are introduced on roads. His legislation is in response to the "No toll on [[I-95]]" group, which is a Roanoke Rapids-based group that opposes instating a toll on I-95. Opponents of the toll argue that it leads to double taxation, and say it is the first time "the federal government has put tolls on an existing interstate."<ref name="Tolling Bill">{{cite news|title=Butterfield to announce tolling bill|url=http://www.chicagotribune.com/news/sns-mct-butterfield-to-announce-tolling-bill-20120504,0,4387482.story|newspaper=Chicago Tribune|date=4 May 2012}}</ref>

On July 23, 2014, Butterfield introduced the joint resolution [[Approving the location of the National Liberty Monument (H.J.Res. 120; 113th Congress)|Approving the location of a memorial to commemorate the more than 5,000 slaves and free Black persons who fought for independence in the American Revolution]].<ref name=hjres120allactions>{{cite web|title=H.J.Res. 120 - All Actions|url=https://beta.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/house-joint-resolution/120/all-actions|publisher=United States Congress|accessdate=9 September 2014}}</ref> The joint resolution would approve the location of a commemorative work to honor the more than [[Black Patriot (American Revolution)|5,000 slaves and free black persons who fought]] in the [[American Revolution]].<ref name=HillHouseAuthorizes>{{cite news|last1=Marcos|first1=Cristina|title=House authorizes location for American Revolution memorial in D.C.|url=http://thehill.com/blogs/floor-action/house/217018-house-authorizes-location-for-american-revolution-memorial-in-dc|accessdate=9 September 2014|publisher=The Hill|date=8 September 2014}}</ref>

'''George Kenneth "G. K." Butterfield, Jr.''' (born April 27, 1947) is the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|NC|1}}, elected in 2004. He is a member of the [[United States Democratic Party|Democratic Party]]. The district is located in the northeastern corner of the state, stretching from [[Durham, North Carolina|Durham]] to [[Elizabeth City, North Carolina|Elizabeth City]] and including all or parts of 24 counties.

An attorney, he served as Resident Superior Court judge for North Carolina (1988-2000) and as a state Supreme Court justice, the latter from 2001-2004.

==Early life and education==
Butterfield was born and raised in a prominent [[African-American]] family in [[Wilson, North Carolina]], the son of Addie Lourine (née Davis) and George Kenneth Butterfield, both of mixed race.<ref>[http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~battle/reps/butterfield.htm "Butterfield"]</ref> Butterfield's father immigrated to the United States from [[Bermuda]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.csce.gov/index.cfm?FuseAction=ContentRecords.ViewWitness&ContentRecord_id=984&ContentType=D&ContentRecordType=D&ParentType=H&CFID=27817&CFTOKEN=61678793 |title=CSCE :: Testimony :: Hon. G.K. Butterfield Commissioner - Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe |publisher=Csce.gov |date= |accessdate=2010-08-23}}</ref>

Described by the ''[[Washington Post]]'' as an "African-American who appears to be white,"<ref name="why">[http://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/gk-butterfield-d-nc/gIQAA20hDP_topic.html "G.K. Butterfield, (D-N.C.)"], ''Politics'', ''Washington Post'', 23 December 2011, accessed 4 April 2013</ref> Butterfield acknowledges he has European as well as African ancestry, and that he identifies as African American. He has noted that he grew up on the "black side" of town and led [[civil rights]] marches.<ref>[http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2008/12/14/ap-many-insisting-that-ob_n_150846.html AP, "Many insisting that Obama is not black"], ''Huffington Post'', 14 December 2008, accessed 4 April 2013</ref> He is a member of the Congressional Black Caucus.

Butterfield graduated from Charles H. Darden High School. He earned degrees in [[political science]] and [[sociology]] from [[North Carolina Central University|North Carolina Central University (NCCU)]].<ref name=aboutGK/> In 1974, he received a [[Juris Doctor]] degree from the [[NCCU School of Law]].<ref name=aboutGK/>

Butterfield served in the [[United States Army]] from 1968 to 1970.<ref>[http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=b001251 BUTTERFIELD, George Kenneth, Jr. (G.K.), (1947 - )] Biographical Directory of the United States Congress</ref>

==Judicial career==
In 1988, Butterfield was elected as Resident Superior Court judge in the first judicial division. For the next twelve years, he presided over civil and criminal court in 46 counties of North Carolina.<ref name=aboutGK/> In February 2001, he was appointed to the [[North Carolina Supreme Court]] by Governor [[Mike Easley]].<ref name=aboutGK/> In [[North Carolina judicial elections, 2002|2002]], Butterfield lost his seat on the Supreme Court but returned to the Superior Court bench by special appointment of Governor [[Mike Easley]] and served in that position until his retirement in May 2004.<ref name=aboutGK/>

==Political career==
Butterfield was first elected to the House of Representatives in a special election in 2004, to fill the seat of [[Frank Ballance]].

'''George Kenneth "G. K." Butterfield, Jr.''' (born April 27, 1947) is the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|NC|1}}, elected in 2004. He is a member of the [[United States Democratic Party|Democratic Party]]. The district is located in the northeastern corner of the state, stretching from [[Durham, North Carolina|Durham]] to [[Elizabeth City, North Carolina|Elizabeth City]] and including all or parts of 24 counties.

An attorney, he served as Resident Superior Court judge for North Carolina (1988-2000) and as a state Supreme Court justice, the latter from 2001-2004.

==Early life and education==
Butterfield was born and raised in a prominent [[African-American]] family in [[Wilson, North Carolina]], the son of Addie Lourine (née Davis) and George Kenneth Butterfield, both of mixed race.<ref>[http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~battle/reps/butterfield.htm "Butterfield"]</ref> Butterfield's father immigrated to the United States from [[Bermuda]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.csce.gov/index.cfm?FuseAction=ContentRecords.ViewWitness&ContentRecord_id=984&ContentType=D&ContentRecordType=D&ParentType=H&CFID=27817&CFTOKEN=61678793 |title=CSCE :: Testimony :: Hon. G.K. Butterfield Commissioner - Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe |publisher=Csce.gov |date= |accessdate=2010-08-23}}</ref>

Described by the ''[[Washington Post]]'' as an "African-American who appears to be white,"<ref name="why">[http://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/gk-butterfield-d-nc/gIQAA20hDP_topic.html "G.K. Butterfield, (D-N.C.)"], ''Politics'', ''Washington Post'', 23 December 2011, accessed 4 April 2013</ref> Butterfield acknowledges he has European as well as African ancestry, and that he identifies as African American. He has noted that he grew up on the "black side" of town and led [[civil rights]] marches.<ref>[http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2008/12/14/ap-many-insisting-that-ob_n_150846.html AP, "Many insisting that Obama is not black"], ''Huffington Post'', 14 December 2008, accessed 4 April 2013</ref> He is a member of the Congressional Black Caucus.

Butterfield graduated from Charles H. Darden High School. He earned degrees in [[political science]] and [[sociology]] from [[North Carolina Central University|North Carolina Central University (NCCU)]].<ref name=aboutGK/> In 1974, he received a [[Juris Doctor]] degree from the [[NCCU School of Law]].<ref name=aboutGK/>

Butterfield served in the [[United States Army]] from 1968 to 1970.<ref>[http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=b001251 BUTTERFIELD, George Kenneth, Jr. (G.K.), (1947 - )] Biographical Directory of the United States Congress</ref>

==Judicial career==
In 1988, Butterfield was elected as Resident Superior Court judge in the first judicial division. For the next twelve years, he presided over civil and criminal court in 46 counties of North Carolina.<ref name=aboutGK/> In February 2001, he was appointed to the [[North Carolina Supreme Court]] by Governor [[Mike Easley]].<ref name=aboutGK/> In [[North Carolina judicial elections, 2002|2002]], Butterfield lost his seat on the Supreme Court but returned to the Superior Court bench by special appointment of Governor [[Mike Easley]] and served in that position until his retirement in May 2004.<ref name=aboutGK/>

==Political career==
Butterfield was first elected to the House of Representatives in a special election in 2004, to fill the seat of [[Frank Ballance]].

He was appointed to this position by [[Speaker of the United States House of Representatives|Speaker]] [[Nancy Pelosi]] and [[Party whips of the United States House of Representatives|Majority Whip]] [[Jim Clyburn]].<ref name=aboutGK/>

==Political positions==
As a member of the House Committee on Energy and Commerce, G.K. Butterfield advocated for the [[American Clean Energy and Security Act]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thehill.com/special-reports/cap-and-trade-june-2009/8229-poorest-americans-contributing-least-to-climate-change-will-not-be-hurt-by-legislation-to-rectify|title=Poorest Americans, contributing least to climate change, will not be hurt by legislation to rectify|author=Rep. G.K. Butterfield (D-N.C.)|date=06/15/09|publisher=The Hill}}</ref> Butterfield supports "a market-based approach to capping carbon emissions"<ref name="Butterfield">{{cite web|url=http://butterfield.house.gov/index.cfm?sectionid=63&sectiontree=4,63|title=Energy & Global Climate Change|publisher=Congressman G.K. Butterfield Official Website}}</ref> and wants to broaden the United States' sources of energy.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thehill.com/blogs/congress-blog/energy-a-environment/103309-hearing-with-oil-executives-underscores-need-for-energy-overhaul-rep-gk-butterfield|title=Hearing with oil executives underscores need for energy overhaul|date=06/15/10|author=Rep. G.K. Butterfield (D-N.C.)|publisher=The Hill}}</ref> On his website, Butterfield stresses the need to find more clean and domestic sources of energy.<ref name="Butterfield" />

A strong supporter of civil rights, he advocated renewal of the [[Voting Rights Act]] and "introduced a bill calling for the Capitol Visitor's Center to acknowledge the [[Slavery in the United States|slave labor]] used to build the Capitol."<ref name="why"/>

In 2009, Butterfield introduced the ''Don't Let the Bed Bugs Bite Act'' "to assist states in carrying out inspections of lodging facilities, train inspection personnel, contract with a commercial exterminator; educate owners and staff at lodging facilities."<ref name="Afro">http://www.afro.com/sections/news/national/story.htm?storyid=3130</ref> Butterfield also passed H.R. 4252 "To amend the Small Business Act to change the net worth amount under the small business program for socially and economically disadvantaged individuals from $750,000 to $978,722, and for other purposes."<ref name="LOC">http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/query/z?c111:H.R.4253:</ref>
 
Butterfield supported the [[Affordable Health Care Act]], and worked with the Energy and Commerce Committee to help write the legislation.<ref name="Vote-smart">http://www.votesmart.org/speech_detail.php?sc_id=538991&keyword=&phrase=&contain=</ref> During the discussion of the bill in Congress, Butterfield complained about the lack of cooperation from the Republican party.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

Butterfield supports increasing taxes for higher-income families while decreasing taxes for middle and low-income families.<ref name="Vote-smart" />  Although he is an advocate for using government stimulus in order to improve the economy, Butterfield wants to reduce government regulations on the private sector.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

In 2008 [[Planned Parenthood]] gave Butterfield an 80 percent ranking. In 2009 Butterfield supported the interests of [[NARAL Pro-Choice America]] 100 percent of the time.<ref name="Vote-smart">[http://www.votesmart.org/issue_rating_category.php?can_id=41077]</ref>  Butterfield identifies as pro-choice and especially supports legalized abortion when the life of the woman is in danger or if the pregnancy is a result of rape or incest.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

Butterfield has repeatedly voted against defining marriage as being between one man and one woman, voting against the [[Marriage Protection Act]] of 2004 and constitutional marriage amendments in 2004 and 2006. He has voted to ban job discrimination based on sexual orientation, and in 2010 voted for the repeal of [[Don't Ask Don't Tell]].<ref>{{cite web|title=G.K. Butterfield - Gay Marriage|url=http://www.thepoliticalguide.com/Profiles/House/North_Carolina/G.K._Butterfield/Views/Gay_Marriage/|publisher=The Political Guide}}</ref>

Butterfield voted in 2008 against the [[Troubled Asset Relief Program]] (TARP) $700 billion bailout of the financial industry and the $14 billion rescue package for the auto industry.<ref name="WP Butterfield 2012">{{cite web|title=Democrat George Kenneth 'G.K.' Butterfield, Jr.|url=http://www.washingtontimes.com/campaign-2012/candidates/george-kenneth-gk-butterfield-jr-34352/|publisher=Washington Post|accessdate=15 May 2012}}</ref>

In 2011, he voted to extend expiring provisions of the [[PATRIOT Act]]<ref>http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2011/roll036.xml</ref> and voted in favor of the [[National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) for Fiscal Year 2012]].<ref>{{cite web|title=HR 1540 National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2012 - Voting Record|url=http://www.votesmart.org/bill/votes/37467|work=Project Vote Smart|accessdate=5 April 2012}}</ref>

In 2012, Butterfield introduced legislation that, if passed, would require more input from the public before tolls are introduced on roads. His legislation is in response to the "No toll on [[I-95]]" group, which is a Roanoke Rapids-based group that opposes instating a toll on I-95. Opponents of the toll argue that it leads to double taxation, and say it is the first time "the federal government has put tolls on an existing interstate."<ref name="Tolling Bill">{{cite news|title=Butterfield to announce tolling bill|url=http://www.chicagotribune.com/news/sns-mct-butterfield-to-announce-tolling-bill-20120504,0,4387482.story|newspaper=Chicago Tribune|date=4 May 2012}}</ref>

On July 23, 2014, Butterfield introduced the joint resolution [[Approving the location of the National Liberty Monument (H.J.Res. 120; 113th Congress)|Approving the location of a memorial to commemorate the more than 5,000 slaves and free Black persons who fought for independence in the American Revolution]].<ref name=hjres120allactions>{{cite web|title=H.J.Res. 120 - All Actions|url=https://beta.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/house-joint-resolution/120/all-actions|publisher=United States Congress|accessdate=9 September 2014}}</ref> The joint resolution would approve the location of a commemorative work to honor the more than [[Black Patriot (American Revolution)|5,000 slaves and free black persons who fought]] in the [[American Revolution]].<ref name=HillHouseAuthorizes>{{cite news|last1=Marcos|first1=Cristina|title=House authorizes location for American Revolution memorial in D.C.|url=http://thehill.com/blogs/floor-action/house/217018-house-authorizes-location-for-american-revolution-memorial-in-dc|accessdate=9 September 2014|publisher=The Hill|date=8 September 2014}}</ref>

==Political campaigns==

===2004===
Butterfield was elected to Congress in a special election on July 20, 2004 (see above.) to fill the unexpired term of Representative [[Frank Ballance]], who resigned for legal reasons. He defeated [[United States Republican Party|Republican]] candidate Greg Dority and [[United States Libertarian Party|Libertarian Party]] nominee Tom Eisenmenger. Butterfield was sworn into office on July 21, 2004.

On July 20, 2004, Butterfield won the Democratic primary entitling him to run in the November 2004 general election. Running against Dority again, he won his first full term with 64% of the popular vote.<ref>{{cite news|author=William L.

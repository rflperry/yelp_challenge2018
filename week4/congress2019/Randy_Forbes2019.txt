'''James Randy Forbes''' (born February 17, 1952) is an American politician.  A member of the [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican Party]], he was the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|VA|4}}, serving from 2001 to 2017.

Prior to joining the [[United States Congress]], he was a member of the [[Virginia House of Delegates]], [[Virginia State Senate]], and Chairman of the [[Republican Party of Virginia]]. Forbes formerly served as Chairman of the Seapower and Projection Forces Subcommittee of the [[House Armed Services Committee]].

During the [[Donald Trump]] administration, Forbes has been reviewed as a prospective choice for [[United States Secretary of the Navy|Secretary of the Navy]].<ref>[http://nationalinterest.org/blog/the-buzz/why-so-many-national-security-experts-want-randy-forbes-18367 Why So Many National Security Experts Want Randy Forbes as Secretary of the Navy], [[The National Interest]], 2016-11-10</ref><ref>[http://www.defensenews.com/articles/interview-us-rep-joe-wilson Interview: US Rep. Joe Wilson], [[Defense News]], 2016-12-13</ref><ref>[http://thehill.com/blogs/pundits-blog/defense/314491-why-theres-only-one-choice-for-trumps-navy-secretary Why there's only one choice for Trump's Navy secretary], [[The Hill (newspaper)|The Hill]], 2017-1-16</ref><ref>[http://www.atimes.com/article/trump-asia-need-randy-forbes-us-navy-secretary/ Why Trump, and Asia, need Randy Forbes as US Navy secretary], [[Asia Times]], 2017-1-16</ref><ref>[https://www.wsj.com/articles/trumps-navy-choice-1488317240 Trump’s Navy Choice: A Secretary who knows Congress would help get to a 350-ship fleet.], [[The Wall Street Journal]], 2017-2-28</ref><ref>[http://breakingdefense.com/2017/03/34918/ Randy Forbes Still A Long Shot For Navy Secretary After Bilden’s Withdrawal], Breaking Defense, 2017-3-1</ref> Forbes campaigned for Trump in the 2016 presidential election in 2016. Forbes was passed over twice for the first-round and second round nominations of Secretary of the Navy.<ref>[https://news.usni.org/2017/02/26/philip-bilden-withdraws-secnav Bilden was formally nominated as Navy Secretary on Jan. 25 after back-and-forth reports in the media as to whether he or former congressman Randy Forbes would get the job.]</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=http://thehill.com/policy/defense/336187-trump-to-nominate-richard-spencer-for-navy-secretary|title=Trump to nominate Richard Spencer for Navy secretary|last=Byrnes|first=Jesse|date=2017-06-02|work=TheHill|access-date=2017-06-03}}</ref>

Forbes served as a senior distinguished fellow at the [[U.S. Naval War College]] from February through December 2017.<ref>[https://www.usnwc.edu/About/News/February-2017/Former-Rep-Randy-Forbes-joins-U-S--Naval-War-Colle.aspx Former Representative Randy Forbes joins Naval War College faculty], [[U.S. Naval War College]], 2017-2-14</ref>

==Early life, education and career==
Forbes was born in [[Chesapeake, Virginia]], the son of Thelma and Malcolm J. Forbes.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~battle/reps/forbes.htm|title=forbes|website=Freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com|accessdate=2016-11-22}}</ref> Forbes graduated first in his class from [[Randolph-Macon College]] in 1974.

He received his [[Juris Doctor]] from the [[University of Virginia School of Law]] in 1977. Forbes worked in private practice for Kaufman & Canoles PC.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Stamper|first1=Megan|title=Meet the Candidates: Rep. Randy Forbes|url=http://pilotonline.com/inside-business/news/municipal-government/meet-the-candidates-rep-randy-forbes/article_c9837f29-b073-506f-84ea-e2ed00ffde44.html|accessdate=April 29, 2016|work=Inside Business|date=October 12, 2012}}</ref>

==Political career==
Forbes served in the [[Virginia House of Delegates]] from 1989 to 1997 and the [[Virginia State Senate]] from 1997 to 2001. He was first elected to the House in 2001 to fill a vacancy caused by the death of ten-term [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic]] Congressman [[Norman Sisisky]]; defeating Democratic State Senator [[Louise Lucas]] 52–48%.<ref>[http://www.sbe.state.va.us/web_docs/election/results/2001/jun2001/June19] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20050307021246/http://www.sbe.state.va.us/web_docs/election/results/2001/jun2001/June19|date=March 7, 2005}}</ref> After the 4th district was [[Redistricting|reconfigured]] as part of redistricting. He ran unopposed by Democrats in 2002 and 2006. In 2004, he faced Jonathan R. Menefee, and won with 65% of the vote.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/elections/2004/va|newspaper=The Washington Post|title=Virginia election results 2004}}</ref> He faced [[Wynne LeGrow]] in the 2010 election, and was easily re-elected with 62% of the vote. In 2012, he defeated Chesapeake City Councilwoman Ella Ward with 57% of the vote.{{citation needed|date=December 2016}}

Forbes was the Founder and Chairman of the [[Congressional Prayer Caucus]] and the Congressional China Caucus.  He championed a plan to rebuild the Navy to 350 ships as Chairman of the House Seapower Subcommittee.

Forbes was Chairman of the [[Republican Party of Virginia]] from 1996 to 2001. On February 8, 2016, he announced that he would run for election to [[Virginia's 2nd Congressional District]] in November 2016, after the 4th District, where he had previously held office, was redrawn to cover most of the majority-black areas in and around Richmond. The 2nd District was vacated by [[Scott Rigell]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://pilotonline.com/news/government/politics/virginia/randy-forbes-switching-districts-in-congressional-election/article_d419f663-2871-54d5-be16-5ae828345390.html|work=The Virginian-Pilot|title=Randy Forbes switching districts in 2016 congressional election}}</ref>

Forbes accused state Delegate and former U.S. Navy SEAL, [[Scott Taylor (politician)|Scott Taylor]], of criminal activity for speeding violations and missing a court appearance, including a scheduled hearing when Taylor was deployed with the Navy.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.politifact.com/virginia/statements/2016/may/23/randy-forbes/randy-forbes-tells-half-story-about-scott-taylors-/|title=Randy Forbes tells half the story about Scott Taylor's court record|work=@politifact|access-date=2017-03-06|language=en}}</ref> Taylor described Forbes as a "coward".<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.militarytimes.com/story/military/capitol-hill/2016/02/22/gop-backers-defense-budget-hike-got-millions-donations/80773518/|title=gop-backers-defense-budget-hike-got-millions-donations|last=Times|first=Military|website=Military Times|language=en|access-date=2017-03-06}}</ref> On June 14, 2016, Forbes was defeated in the Republican primary by Scott Taylor by a margin of 52.5% to 40.6%, with a third candidate, C. Pat Cardwell IV, receiving 6.8% of the vote. Taylor went on to win the general election on November 8, 2016.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://pilotonline.com/news/government/politics/virginia/scott-taylor-defeats-veteran-randy-forbes-in-nd-congressional-primary/article_2e1315de-1d5a-51fb-801a-be2886af5e02.html|work=The Virginian-Pilot|title=Scott Taylor defeats veteran Randy Forbes in 2nd Congressional primary thanks to feisty grassroots campaign|accessdate=December 3, 2016}}</ref>

Forbes received $801,606 in campaign financing from donors in the defense industry during his tenure in Congress.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.militarytimes.com/story/military/capitol-hill/2016/02/22/gop-backers-defense-budget-hike-got-millions-donations/80773518/|title=gop-backers-defense-budget-hike-got-millions-donations|last=Times|first=Military|website=Military Times|language=en|access-date=2017-03-06}}</ref>  The largest donors to Forbes over his Congressional career have been defense contractors serving the U.S. Navy for aviation and ship construction, including Northrup Grumman, BAE Systems, Leidos and Huntington Ingalls.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.opensecrets.org/politicians/contrib.php?cycle=Career&cid=N00013799&type=I|title=Rep. Randy Forbes: Campaign Finance/Money - Top Donors - Representative Career {{!}} OpenSecrets|website=www.opensecrets.org|language=en|access-date=2017-03-06}}</ref>

In 2013, Forbes publicly opposed military action in both Libya and Syria.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://forbes.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=347488 |title=Forbes Releases Statement Opposing Intervention in Syria - Congressman J. ===China===
Forbes was founder and chairman of the Congressional China Caucus.<ref>{{cite web|title = About the Caucus|url = https://forbes.house.gov/chinacaucus/about.htm|website = forbes.house.gov|access-date = 2016-01-22}}</ref> Forbes spoke a panel discussion at [[Harvard University]] in the April 2012 on U.S. strategy to China's world power emergence.<ref>{{cite web|title = Forbes to Speak Tomorrow at Harvard on U.S.-China Relations|url = http://forbes.house.gov/updates/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=289736|website = forbes.house.gov|access-date = 2016-01-22}}</ref> Forbes has voiced concern for [[Chinese intelligence operations in the United States|Chinese military ambition]], [[Cyberwarfare in China|cyber threats]], [[Pollution in China|contaminated exports]], and [[Human rights in China|human rights violations]]. His reputation has come under scrutiny with the recent acquisition of America's largest pork company, [[Smithfield Foods]], by a Chinese competitor – a company headquartered within his district. This $4.7 billion deal is the biggest Chinese acquisition of a U.S. company to date.<ref>{{cite web|title = Who's behind the Chinese takeover of world's biggest pork producer?|url = https://www.pbs.org/newshour/bb/whos-behind-chinese-takeover-worlds-biggest-pork-producer/|website = PBS NewsHour|access-date = 2016-01-22|language = en-US}}</ref>

===Energy independence===
On June 12, 2008 Forbes introduced H.R. 6260, titled "[[New Manhattan Project for Energy Independence]]". The bill was offered as a substitute for the entire energy bill and outlined a series of prizes, similar to the [[X-PRIZE]], which would be awarded to a private entity, which completed one of seven tasks related to achieving [[United States energy independence|energy independence]].

The bill included $14 billion in prizes and $10 billion in grants ($10 billion of which would have supported [[nuclear fusion]] research); provisions to establish a summit to discuss the challenge of energy independence; and creation of a commission to offer recommendations to fulfill the goal of becoming energy independent within 20 years. On June 26, 2009, the bill was offered as an amendment in the nature of a substitute for the Waxman/Markey-sponsored [[American Clean Energy and Security Act]]. The amendment was rejected by the House of Representatives 255–172.<ref>{{cite news|last=Bartel|first=Bill|title=Forbes' GOP alternative to climate bill shot down|url=http://hamptonroads.com/2009/06/forbes-gop-alternative-climate-bill-shot-down|newspaper=The Virginian-Pilot|date=June 27, 2009}}</ref>

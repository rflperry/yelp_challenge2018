'''George Kenneth Butterfield Jr.''' (born April 27, 1947) is a United States Congressman elected in 2004. He is a member of the [[United States Democratic Party|Democratic Party]]. His district is located in the northeastern corner of [[North Carolina]], stretching from [[Durham, North Carolina|Durham]] to [[Elizabeth City, North Carolina|Elizabeth City]] and including all or parts of 24 counties. An African American and a longtime advocate on behalf of civil rights, Butterfield is a member of the [[Congressional Black Caucus]],<ref>{{cite web|title=Membership|author=|url=https://cbc.house.gov/membership/|format=|publisher=Congressional Black Caucus|date=|accessdate=7 March 2018}}</ref> and served as its chair from 2015 to 2017.

==Early life and education==
Butterfield was born and raised in [[Wilson, North Carolina]], in a prominent African American family. He is the son of Addie Lourine Davis and George Kenneth Butterfield, both of whom were [[Multiracial Americans|mixed race Americans]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~battle/reps/butterfield.htm|title=g k butterfield|publisher=|accessdate=3 April 2017}}</ref> His father was an [[Bermudian Americans|immigrant from Bermuda]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.csce.gov/index.cfm?FuseAction=ContentRecords.ViewWitness&ContentRecord_id=984&ContentType=D&ContentRecordType=D&ParentType=H&CFID=27817&CFTOKEN=61678793 |title=CSCE :: Testimony :: Hon. G.K. Butterfield Commissioner - Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe |publisher=Csce.gov |accessdate=2010-08-23}}</ref>

In describing his racial identity as a black man, he has pointed to his African heritage, while also noting that he grew up on the "black side" of town in racially segregated North Carolina and led [[civil rights]] marches.<ref>[http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2008/12/14/ap-many-insisting-that-ob_n_150846.html AP, "Many insisting that Obama is not black"], ''Huffington Post'', 14 December 2008, accessed 4 April 2013</ref> He is the former chair of the Congressional Black Caucus.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://butterfield.house.gov/about/committees-and-caucuses|title=Committees & Caucuses|date=8 August 2014 |accessdate=3 April 2017}}</ref>

Butterfield graduated from Charles H. Darden High School. He earned degrees in [[political science]] and [[sociology]] from [[North Carolina Central University|North Carolina Central University (NCCU)]].<ref name=aboutGK/> In 1974, he received a [[Juris Doctor]] degree from the [[NCCU School of Law]].<ref name=aboutGK/>

Butterfield served in the [[United States Army]] from 1968 to 1970.<ref>[http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=b001251 BUTTERFIELD, George Kenneth, Jr. (G.K.), (1947 - )] Biographical Directory of the United States Congress</ref>

==Judicial career==
In 1988, Butterfield was elected as Resident [[North Carolina Superior Court|Superior Court]] judge in the first judicial division. For the next twelve years, he presided over civil and criminal court in 46 counties of North Carolina.<ref name=aboutGK/> In February 2001, he was appointed to the [[North Carolina Supreme Court]] by Governor [[Mike Easley]].<ref name=aboutGK/> In [[North Carolina judicial elections, 2002|2002]], Butterfield lost his seat on the Supreme Court but returned to the Superior Court bench by special appointment of Governor [[Mike Easley]] and served in that position until his retirement in May 2004.<ref name=aboutGK/>

==Political career==
Butterfield was first elected to the House of Representatives in a special election in 2004, to fill the seat of [[Frank Ballance]], who resigned.

Butterfield serves on the [[U.S. House Committee on Energy and Commerce|House Committee on Energy and Commerce]], and formerly served on the [[U.S. House Committee on Armed Services|House Armed Services Committee]] and the [[U.S. House Committee on Agriculture|House Agriculture Committee]]. He is the Region VIII representative on the [[Steering and Policy Committee of the United States House of Representatives|House Democratic Steering and Policy Committee]].

Beginning in 2007 in the [[110th Congress]], Butterfield was chosen to serve as one of eight Chief Deputy Whips; they assist in the formulation of Democratic policy and ensure the passage of legislation by maintaining good communication with members. He was appointed to this position by [[Speaker of the United States House of Representatives|Speaker]] [[Nancy Pelosi]] and [[Party whips of the United States House of Representatives|Majority Whip]] [[Jim Clyburn]].<ref name=aboutGK/>

==Political positions==
As a member of the House Committee on Energy and Commerce, G.K. Butterfield advocated for the [[American Clean Energy and Security Act]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thehill.com/special-reports/cap-and-trade-june-2009/8229-poorest-americans-contributing-least-to-climate-change-will-not-be-hurt-by-legislation-to-rectify|title=Poorest Americans, contributing least to climate change, will not be hurt by legislation to rectify|author=Rep. G.K. Butterfield (D-N.C.)|date=June 15, 2009|publisher=The Hill}}</ref> Butterfield supports "a market-based approach to capping carbon emissions"<ref name="Butterfield">{{cite web|url=http://butterfield.house.gov/index.cfm?sectionid=63&sectiontree=4,63 |title=Energy & Global Climate Change |publisher=Congressman G.K. Butterfield Official Website |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20101210164934/http://butterfield.house.gov/index.cfm?sectionid=63&sectiontree=4,63 |archivedate=2010-12-10 |df= }}</ref> and wants to broaden the United States' sources of energy.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thehill.com/blogs/congress-blog/energy-a-environment/103309-hearing-with-oil-executives-underscores-need-for-energy-overhaul-rep-gk-butterfield|title=Hearing with oil executives underscores need for energy overhaul|date=June 15, 2010|author=Rep. G.K. Butterfield (D-N.C.)|publisher=The Hill}}</ref> On his website, Butterfield stresses the need to find more clean and domestic sources of energy.<ref name="Butterfield" />

A strong supporter of civil rights, he advocated renewal of the [[Voting Rights Act]] and "introduced a bill calling for the Capitol Visitor's Center to acknowledge the [[Slavery in the United States|slave labor]] used to build the Capitol."<ref name="why">[https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/gk-butterfield-d-nc/gIQAA20hDP_topic.html "G.K. Butterfield, (D-N.C.)"], ''Politics'', ''Washington Post'', 23 December 2011, accessed 4 April 2013</ref>

Originally endorsing [[John Edwards]] for the 2008 presidential primary,<ref>[http://projects.newsobserver.com/under_the_dome/butterfield_now_endorses_obama Butterfield now endorses Obama] {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080524020228/http://projects.newsobserver.com/under_the_dome/butterfield_now_endorses_obama |date=May 24, 2008 }}</ref> in January 2008, Butterfield endorsed [[Barack Obama]] for the primary and general election.<ref>{{cite news|last=Boyer|first=Robert|title=Hunt among state Dems stumping for Obama|newspaper=Times-News|date=2008-10-12|url=http://www.thetimesnews.com/news/hunt_18596___article.html/obama_state.html|deadurl=yes|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20081014034811/http://www.thetimesnews.com/news/hunt_18596___article.html/obama_state.html|archivedate=2008-10-14|df=}}</ref>

In 2009, Butterfield introduced the ''Don't Let the Bed Bugs Bite Act'' "to assist states in carrying out inspections of lodging facilities, train inspection personnel, contract with a commercial exterminator; educate owners and staff at lodging facilities."<ref name="Afro">http://www.afro.com/sections/news/national/story.htm?storyid=3130</ref> Butterfield also passed H.R. 4252 "To amend the Small Business Act to change the net worth amount under the small business program for socially and economically disadvantaged individuals from $750,000 to $978,722, and for other purposes."<ref name="LOC">{{cite web|url=http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/query/z?c111:H.R.4253:|title=Text - H.R.4253 - 111th Congress (2009-2010): To amend the Small Business Act to change the net worth amount under the small business program for socially and economically disadvantaged individuals from $750,000 to $978,722, and for other purposes.|first=Butterfield,|last=G.|date=9 December 2009|website=thomas.loc.gov|accessdate=6 February 2018}}</ref>
 
Butterfield supported the [[Affordable Care Act]], and worked with the Energy and Commerce Committee to help write the legislation.<ref name="Vote-smart">{{cite web |url=http://www.votesmart.org/issue_rating_category.php?can_id=41077|title=The Voter's Self Defense System|publisher=|accessdate=3 April 2017}}</ref> During the discussion of the bill in Congress, Butterfield complained about the lack of cooperation from the Republican party.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

Butterfield supports increasing taxes for higher-income families while decreasing taxes for middle and low-income families.<ref name="Vote-smart" /> Although he is an advocate for using government stimulus in order to improve the economy, Butterfield wants to reduce government regulations on the private sector.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

In 2008 [[Planned Parenthood]] gave Butterfield an 80 percent ranking. In 2009 Butterfield supported the interests of [[NARAL Pro-Choice America]] 100 percent of the time.<ref name="Vote-smart" /> Butterfield identifies as pro-choice and especially supports legalized abortion when the life of the woman is in danger or if the pregnancy is a result of rape or incest.<ref name="Vote-smart" />

Butterfield has repeatedly voted against defining marriage as being between one man and one woman, voting against the [[Marriage Protection Act]] of 2004 and constitutional marriage amendments in 2004 and 2006. He has voted to ban job discrimination based on sexual orientation, and in 2010 voted for the repeal of [[Don't Ask Don't Tell]].<ref>{{cite web|title=G.K. Butterfield - Gay Marriage|url=http://www.thepoliticalguide.com/Profiles/House/North_Carolina/G.K._Butterfield/Views/Gay_Marriage/|publisher=The Political Guide}}</ref>

[[File:Hillary Clinton and Congressman GK Butterfield (25567560032).jpg|thumb|Butterfield and [[Hillary Clinton]] at [[Hillside High School (Durham, North Carolina)|Hillside High School]] in [[Durham, North Carolina]], March 2016]]
Butterfield voted in 2008 against the [[Troubled Asset Relief Program]] (TARP) $700 billion bailout of the financial industry and the $14 billion rescue package for the auto industry.<ref name="WP Butterfield 2012">{{cite web|title=Democrat George Kenneth 'G.K.' Butterfield, Jr. |url=http://www.washingtontimes.com/campaign-2012/candidates/george-kenneth-gk-butterfield-jr-34352/ |publisher=Washington Post |accessdate=15 May 2012 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20131224094407/http://www.washingtontimes.com/campaign-2012/candidates/george-kenneth-gk-butterfield-jr-34352/ |archivedate=24 December 2013 |df= }}</ref>

In 2011, he voted to extend expiring provisions of the [[PATRIOT Act]]<ref>http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2011/roll036.xml</ref> and voted in favor of the [[National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) for Fiscal Year 2012]].<ref>{{cite web|title=HR 1540 National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2012 - Voting Record|url=http://www.votesmart.org/bill/votes/37467|work=Project Vote Smart|accessdate=5 April 2012}}</ref>

In 2012, Butterfield introduced legislation that, if passed, would require more input from the public before tolls are introduced on roads. His legislation is in response to the "No toll on [[I-95]]" group, which is a Roanoke Rapids-based group that opposes instating a toll on I-95. Opponents of the toll argue that it leads to double taxation, and say it is the first time "the federal government has put tolls on an existing interstate."<ref name="Tolling Bill">{{cite news|title=Butterfield to announce tolling bill|url=http://www.chicagotribune.com/news/sns-mct-butterfield-to-announce-tolling-bill-20120504,0,4387482.story|newspaper=Chicago Tribune|date=4 May 2012}}</ref>

On July 23, 2014, Butterfield introduced the joint resolution [[Approving the location of the National Liberty Monument (H.J.Res. 120; 113th Congress)|approving the location of a memorial to commemorate the more than 5,000 slaves and free Black persons who fought for independence in the American Revolution]].<ref name=hjres120allactions>{{cite web|title=H.J.Res. 120 - All Actions|url=https://beta.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/house-joint-resolution/120/all-actions|publisher=United States Congress|accessdate=9 September 2014}}</ref> The joint resolution would approve the location of a commemorative work to honor the more than [[Black Patriot (American Revolution)|5,000 slaves and free black persons who fought]] in the [[American Revolution]].<ref name=HillHouseAuthorizes>{{cite news|last1=Marcos|first1=Cristina|title=House authorizes location for American Revolution memorial in D.C.|url=http://thehill.com/blogs/floor-action/house/217018-house-authorizes-location-for-american-revolution-memorial-in-dc|accessdate=9 September 2014|publisher=The Hill|date=8 September 2014}}</ref>

==Political campaigns==

===2004===
Butterfield was elected to Congress in a special election on July 20, 2004 to fill the unexpired term of Representative [[Frank Ballance]], who resigned for health reasons. He defeated [[United States Republican Party|Republican]] candidate Greg Dority and [[United States Libertarian Party|Libertarian Party]] nominee Tom Eisenmenger. Butterfield was sworn into office on July 21, 2004.

On July 20, 2004, Butterfield won the Democratic primary entitling him to run in the November 2004 general election. Running against Dority again, he won his first full term with 64% of the popular vote.<ref>{{cite news|author=William L. Holmes|title=Butterfield Wins Special Election; Will Face Dority in November|date=21 July 2004|publisher=Associated Press}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|author=Cindy George|title=Former Justice Wins 1st District; Butterfield Fills Ballance’s Seat|date=21 July 2004|work=News and Observer|page=A16}}</ref>

===2006===
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in North Carolina, 2006}}
Butterfield was unopposed for reelection in 2006.

===2008===
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in North Carolina, 2008#District 1}}
Butterfield won against Dean Stephens with 70.28% of the vote.<ref>{{cite news|title=North Carolina Election Results 2008|publisher=New York Times |url=http://elections.nytimes.com/2008/results/states/north-carolina.html|accessdate=2008-11-06}}</ref>

===2010===
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in North Carolina, 2010#District 1}}
Butterfield defeated Republican nominee [[Ashley Woolard]].

===2012===
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in North Carolina, 2012#District 1}}
[[File:G. K. Butterfield 2012 DNC day 3 (7959882550) (cropped2).jpg|thumb|left|Butterfield speaking at the [[2012 Democratic National Convention]]]]
Butterfield sought re-election in 2012; the district was expected to strongly favor Democrats.<ref name="GOP Gains">{{cite news|last=Miller|first=Joshua|title=Race Ratings: GOP Looks for Major Gains in North Carolina|url=http://www.rollcall.com/issues/57_21/Race-Ratings-GOP-Looks-for-Major-Gains-North-Carolina-208044-1.html|accessdate=15 May 2012|newspaper=Roll Call|date=8 August 2011}}</ref>

In April 2012, Butterfield accompanied President Obama to speak at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill to speak about extending the current interest rates on federal loan program for many undergraduate students. Butterfield expressed concern with the pending expiration, saying: “Allowing the current interest rates to expire would burden students with additional debt, prolong their ability to kick start their careers, and send the message that it is more important to cut taxes for the wealthy than educational expenses for our young people.”<ref name="CH Speech">{{cite news|last=Johnston|first=Bill|title=Butterfield to Join President Obama at Chapel Hill Speech Today|url=http://goldsborodailynews.com/2012/04/24/butterfield-to-join-president-obama-at-chapel-hill-speech-today/|accessdate=15 May 2012|newspaper=Goldsboro Daily News|date=24 April 2012}}</ref>

==Personal life==
In 1971, Butterfield married [[Jean Farmer-Butterfield|Jean Farmer]]. They have two daughters, [[Valeisha Butterfield Jones|Valeisha]] and Lenai, who are now adults.<ref name=aboutGK/> He and Jean divorced in 1991. Jean has been a member of the [[North Carolina House of Representatives]] representing House District 24 since 2003. Their daughter Valeisha is married to NBA player [[Dahntay Jones]]. They have one child together, Dahntay Jr. In addition, Butterfield is the father of an adult daughter, Tunya Michelle Butterfield Smith. Tunya and her husband, Chris Smith, have twin sons, Gavin and Chase.

G.K. Butterfield is a lifelong member of Jackson Chapel First Missionary Baptist Church in [[Wilson, North Carolina]], where he has served as Trustee and Chairman of the Finance Ministry.<ref name=aboutGK>{{cite web|title=About GK|publisher=[[United States House of Representatives]]|url=http://butterfield.house.gov/aboutgk.asp|accessdate=2008-11-05|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20080730222702/http://butterfield.house.gov/aboutgk.asp|archivedate=2008-07-30|deadurl=yes}}</ref> Butterfield serves as a Trustee of Gallaudet University. He is also a member of [[Groove Phi Groove]] Social Fellowship, Incorporated.<ref>{{cite web|last=Davis|first=Edmond|title=Groove Phi Groove Social Fellowship (1962- )|url=http://www.blackpast.org/?q=aah/groove-phi-groove-social-fellowship-1962 |publisher=BlackPast.org|accessdate=11 December 2011}}</ref>

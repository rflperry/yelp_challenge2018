'''Robert A. Brady''' (born April 7, 1945) is an [[United States|American politician]] who has been the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|PA|1|}} since 1998. He is the [[ranking member|ranking]] [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democrat]] on the [[United States House Committee on House Administration]] since 2007. He has served as chairman of the Philadelphia Democratic Party for more than 30 years<ref name=":1">{{Cite news|url=https://www.politico.com/story/2018/01/31/bob-brady-no-reelection-380232|title=Rep. Robert Brady not running for reelection|work=POLITICO|access-date=2018-09-17|language=en}}</ref>, and is one of the few members of Congress who is also a county chairman (Philadelphia Democratic City Committee).

In January 2018, Brady announced that he will not run for re-election in [[United States House of Representatives elections in Pennsylvania, 2018|2018]].<ref>{{Cite web | url=http://www.philly.com/philly/news/politics/rep-robert-brady-not-running-reelection-pennsylvania-20180131.html | title=Bob Brady is not Running for Reelection | first=Holly | last=Otterbein | work=[[Philadelphia Inquirer]] | date=January 31, 2018 | accessdate=January 31, 2018}}</ref>

==Early life, education, and pre-congressional career==
Brady was born in [[Philadelphia]], the son of Enez (née Caterini) and Robert G. Brady, a [[police officer]]. His father was of [[Irish American|Irish]] descent, and his maternal grandparents were immigrants from [[Italian American|Italy]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.whorunsgov.com/Profiles/Robert_A._Brady|title=Robert Brady profile|website=whorunsgov.com|accessdate=1 June 2017|deadurl=yes|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20111105202401/http://www.whorunsgov.com/Profiles/Robert_A._Brady|archivedate=5 November 2011|df=}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~battle/reps/bradybob.htm|title=Bob Brady ancestry|website=freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com|accessdate=1 June 2017}}</ref> He graduated from [[St. Thomas More High School]], but did not attend college, instead going to work as a [[carpentry|carpenter]]. He became a part of the leadership of the union, and remains a member of several unions.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.brady.house.gov/index.cfm?sectionid=117&sectiontree=2,117|title=Robert Brady House of Representatives website|website=house.gov|accessdate=1 June 2017}}</ref>

He was elected a precinct committeeman for the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]] in 1968. When ward leader and then-City Council President [[George X. Schwartz]] was convicted and imprisoned in the [[Abscam]] scandal, Brady was elected to succeed him as Democratic Leader of the 34th Ward, a position that he still holds today.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.seventy.org/Downloads/2009_Citizen's_Guide.pdf|title=2009 Citizen's Guide|accessdate=2009-12-21|last=[[Committee of Seventy]]|date=2009-12-21|format=PDF|work=2009 Citizen's Guide|publisher=The Committee of Seventy, Philadelphia, PA 19103|deadurl=yes|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20090419063456/http://seventy.org/Downloads/2009_Citizen%27s_Guide.pdf|archivedate=2009-04-19|df=}}</ref> Since June 1986, Brady has been the Chairman of the Philadelphia Democratic Party. He served as a staff aide in the [[Philadelphia City Council]] and a staff aide in the [[Pennsylvania State Senate]]. Brady has been an instructor at the [[University of Pennsylvania]]. He was also a member of the [[Pennsylvania Turnpike Commission]] from 1991 until his election as a Congressman.

===Elections===
{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Pennsylvania, 2012#District 1}}
When 17-year U.S. Congressman [[Tom Foglietta]] resigned from the [[United States House of Representatives|House of Representatives]] upon being named [[U.S. Ambassador]] to [[Italy]] by [[Bill Clinton]], Brady was unopposed in the primary and won the general election with 74% of the vote. Brady was sworn in on May 19, 1998. He has won re-election since, with at least 81% of the vote. An 18-year veteran in Congress in 2016, Brady was elected to his 10th full term over Republican challenger Deborah Williams in the general election on November 8, 2016, with his 81% of the vote record intact.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.delcotimes.com/article/DC/20161105/NEWS/161109783|title=What's at stake as Delco voters cast ballots on Election Day|access-date=2016-11-11}}</ref> Brady represents a heavily Democratic area and is the head of the Philadelphia Democratic Party, thus, he has generally won the Democratic nomination in each election unopposed.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.phillyvoice.com/2016-general-election-Pennsylvania-congressional-results/|title=Meehan, Fitzpatrick win tough U.S. House races in Pennsylvania|date=2016-11-09|newspaper=PhillyVoice|access-date=2016-11-11}}</ref> Deborah Williams also ran against Brady in the 2004 race, and in that race she received the second highest number of votes a Republican candidate has ever achieved against the incumbent, which she topped in the 2016 election.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.phillyvoice.com/phillys-toughest-congressional-candidate-female-black-and-republican/|title=Philly's toughest Congressional candidate? Female, black and Republican|date=2016-11-02|newspaper=PhillyVoice|access-date=2016-11-11}}</ref> The 2016 election marks the most votes any challenger has ever received against him.

====Corruption case====
In November 2017, it was revealed that the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) was investigating Brady for alleged payments that Brady's campaign made in 2012 to an opponent in order to get the opponent to withdraw from the race.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.cbsnews.com/news/draft-court-docs-congressman-accused-of-conspiracy-false-statements/|title=Court docs: Congressman Bob Brady under FBI scrutiny in campaign finance probe|access-date=2017-11-21|language=en}}</ref> In January 2018, he announced that he would not seek reelection.<ref name=":0">{{Cite news|url=https://apnews.com/ef82d0b6e312445eb1856a07c6cce417|title=Philly congressman Brady won't seek another term|work=AP News|access-date=2018-02-01|language=en-US}}</ref> He said that his decision had nothing to do with the ongoing corruption probe, but that he instead wanted to spend more time with his family.<ref name=":0" />

=== Political positions ===
Brady is pro-choice and voted against President Bush's restrictions on funding to family planning groups in 2001. Over the years, he has supported minors' abortion rights, voting in 2005 for the right for those under 18 years of age to obtain abortions across state lines without parental consent.<ref name="ontheissues">[http://www.ontheissues.org/PA/Robert_Brady_Abortion.htm], Robert Brady on Abortion. Retrieved November 23, 2010.</ref> He voted against the Stupak-Pitts Amendment, which prohibits health insurance companies from offering abortion coverage in a plan to any citizen. He opposed a proposal to prohibit federally funded abortion services.<ref name="thatsmycongress: an independent voice on campaigns and legislations">[http://thatsmycongress.com/house/repBradyPA1111.html], Representative Robert Brady. Retrieved November 23, 2010.</ref> Over the past fifteen years, organizations such as the National Association for the Repeal of Abortion Laws (NARAL), the National Family Planning & Reproductive Health Association, and Planned Parenthood rated him 100%.<ref name="projectvotesmart">[http://www.votesmart.org/issue_rating_category.php?can_id=2519], Interest Group Ratings. Retrieved November 23, 2010.</ref>

Brady voted in favor of the Small Business Lending Fund and Tax Law Amendment. The September 2010 amendment provides loans to small business through financial institutions.<ref>[http://www.votesmart.org/issue_keyvote_detail.php?cs_id=32155&can_id=2519], Key Vote: Small Business Lending Fund and Tax Law Amendments. Retrieved November 23, 2010.</ref> He supported the Small Business Jobs Bill in October 2010.

Brady voted against the Secure Fence Act (2006), which authorized the construction of additional fencing along the U.S.-Mexico border, as well as the Immigration Law Enforcement Act of 2006.<ref>[http://www.votesmart.org/issue_keyvote_detail.php?cs_id=8235&can_id=2519], Key Vote: Secure Fence Act of 2006. Retrieved November 23, 2010.</ref> He supports Homeland Security Appropriations for border protection and The U.S. Visitor and Immigrant Status Technology project, which works towards improving the capability of the government to keep track of immigrants and control their entry and exit.<ref name="EPIC">[http://epic.org/privacy/us-visit/], U.S. VISIT. Retrieved November 24, 2010.</ref> Groups such as the National Latino Congreso/William C. Velásquez Institute, the American Immigration Lawyers Association, and the anti-immigration [[Federation for American Immigration Reform]] have rated Brady between 50% and 100% for his pro-immigration political stances.<ref name="projectvotesmart"/>

==2007 Mayoral campaign==
{{main|2007 Philadelphia Mayoral Election}}
On January 25, 2007 Brady announced that he would seek the Democratic Party nomination for [[mayor of Philadelphia]]. He was the second sitting U.S. Congressman after [[Chaka Fattah]] to announce his candidacy for mayor.<ref>{{cite news|last=Lucey|first=Catherine|title=Starting Today, he's in the race|publisher=Philadelphia Daily News|date=January 25, 2007|url=http://www.thenextmayor.com/vault/20070125Brady.html|accessdate=2007-04-13|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20070927215403/http://www.thenextmayor.com/vault/20070125Brady.html|archivedate=September 27, 2007}}</ref> On March 6, Brady failed to list his city pension on the financial-interests statement he filed with his nominating petitions. Within a week [[Milton Street]] filed a petition challenge to remove Brady from the ballot for failing to disclose his pension income. Another challenge was brought by a group of voters, and supported by mayoral candidates [[Tom Knox]] and Dwight Evans. On April 13, 2007, the [[Commonwealth Court of Pennsylvania|Pennsylvania Commonwealth Court]] ruled that Brady's failure to not list part of his income would not keep him off the primary ballot.<ref>{{cite news|title=Court Ruling Keeps Brady On Mayoral Primary Ballot|agency=Associated Press|date=April 13, 2007|url=http://cbs3.com/topstories/local_story_103165943.html|accessdate=2007-04-13|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20080209140220/http://cbs3.com/topstories/local_story_103165943.html|archivedate=February 9, 2008}}</ref> On May 15, 2007, Brady lost the Democratic mayoral primary to [[Michael Nutter]].

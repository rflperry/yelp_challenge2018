'''David Eugene Price''' (born August 17, 1940) is a [[professor]] and the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|NC|4}}, serving since 1997 and previously from 1987 to 1995. He is a member of the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]]. The district includes portions of, among other cities, [[Burlington, North Carolina|Burlington]], [[Fayetteville, North Carolina|Fayetteville]], [[Raleigh, North Carolina|Raleigh]], [[Durham, North Carolina|Durham]], and all of [[Chapel Hill, North Carolina|Chapel Hill]].

==Early life, education, and academic career==
Born in [[Erwin, Tennessee]], Price attended [[Mars Hill College]] in [[Mars Hill, North Carolina]] when it was a [[junior college]].<ref name="newsobserver">{{cite news
  | title = Dogged Price does homework, pursues goals
  | work = Raleigh News and Observer
  | date = 1994-10-29
  | url =
  | accessdate = 2008-07-03 }}</ref> He later transferred to the [[University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill]], after winning a scholarship, and became a member of [[The Dialectic and Philanthropic Societies]]. He earned a degree in 1961.<ref name="newsobserver"/> Originally intent on becoming an engineer,<ref name="newsobserver"/> Price continued his education at [[Yale University]] where he received a [[theology]] degree (1964) and a Ph.D. in [[political science]] (1969).<ref name="about-david">{{cite web
  | title = About David
  | work = [[United States House of Representatives]]
  | url = http://price.house.gov/about-david
  | accessdate = 2013-10-23 }}</ref>

He served as an aide to [[Alaska]] Senator [[Bob Bartlett]] from 1963 to 1967 and then entered [[academia]], working as a [[political science]] and [[public policy]] professor at [[Duke University]] from 1973 until his first campaign for Congress in 1986.<ref name="about-david"/> He also served as a Duke professor during 1995 and 1996, when he was not in Congress.<ref name="about-david"/>

Price worked for the [[North Carolina Democratic Party]] from 1979 to 1984.<ref name="about-david"/> Price has written a political science textbook entitled ''The Congressional Experience'', from the perspective of a candidate for office, and then, a member of Congress.

Price also served as executive director and then state chair for the North Carolina Democratic Party prior to being elected to Congress.<ref name="newsobserver"/>

==U.S. House of Representatives==

===Elections===
;1986-1992
Price first entered Congress in 1987 after defeating one-term Republican U.S. Congressman [[Bill Cobey]] 56%-44%.<ref name="about-david"/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=36363 |title=NC District 4 Race - Nov 04, 1986 |publisher=Our Campaigns |date= |accessdate=2012-02-10}}</ref> Price won re-election in 1988 and 1990 with 58%.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=37714 |title=NC District 4 Race - Nov 08, 1988 |publisher=Our Campaigns |date= |accessdate=2012-02-10}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=34408 |title=NC District 4 Race - Nov 06, 1990 |publisher=Our Campaigns |date= |accessdate=2012-02-10}}</ref> In 1992, he won re-election with 65%.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=28253 |title=NC District 4 Race - Nov 03, 1992 |publisher=Our Campaigns |date= |accessdate=2012-02-10}}</ref>

;1994
In 1994, he was narrowly defeated by [[United States Republican Party|Republican]] challenger and former [[Raleigh, North Carolina|Raleigh]] police chief [[Fred Heineman]],  who won by a margin of less than 1%<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=28958 |title=NC District 4 Race - Nov 08, 1994 |publisher=Our Campaigns |date= |accessdate=2012-02-10}}</ref> during the [[Republican Revolution]], in part to lower-than-expected turnout in the Democratic stronghold of [[Orange County, North Carolina|Orange County]]{{Citation needed|date=July 2008}} (home to Chapel Hill), but despite the fact that heavily Republican [[Randolph County, North Carolina|Randolph County]] had been eliminated from the Fourth District during redistricting prior to that election year.<ref name="newsobserver"/>

;1996
In 1996, Price defeated Heineman in a rematch 54%-44%.<ref>{{cite web|title=4th Congressional District|url=http://www.app.sboe.state.nc.us/NCSBE/Elec/Results/PastElect/results9/ushouse.htm|work=US House of Representatives election results|publisher=NC State Board of Elections.|accessdate=20 November 2011}}</ref> He was helped in part by voters who were not happy with the lack of progress made by the freshman class on the goals of the [[Contract with America]].<ref>[http://online.wsj.com/public/article/SB116251835022712110-pJlH0UbKjEJxPOgJSQGH_KAvL4o_20061110.html?mod=blogs Wall Street Journal - Republican Rebels of '94 Now Face Their Own Revolt]</ref>

===Tenure===
[[File:David Price NC Congressman 1992.jpg|thumbnail|left|David Price in 1992]]
He was an early opponent of the [[Iraq War of 2003]]<ref>{{cite web|url=http://price.house.gov/list/speech/nc04_price/100902.shtml|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20080528203815/http://price.house.gov/list/speech/nc04_price/100902.shtml|archivedate=23 August 2013|title=An Alternative to the Iraq War Resolution|author=David Price|publisher=[[United States House of Representatives]]|date=9 October 2002}}</ref> and has sponsored a bill to bring the conduct of [[private military companies]] working in Iraq under legal jurisdiction of the United States.<ref>{{cite news|title=House Bill Would Allow Prosecution of Contractors|url=http://www.nytimes.com/2007/10/04/washington/05cong.html|author=David M. Herszenhorn|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=October 4, 2007}}</ref>  He has also introduced legislation to prohibit contractors from performing interrogations of prisoners in the custody of intelligence agencies.<ref>{{cite news|title=Interrogation for Profit|url=http://www.nytimes.com/2008/06/12/opinion/12thu1.html|work=The New York Times|date=June 12, 2008}}</ref>

As chairman of the 2008 House subcommittee responsible for determining the budget for the [[Department of Homeland Security]], Price was seeking to focus immigration enforcement efforts on criminal convicts.<ref>{{cite news|title=Dems raise stakes on immigration|url=http://www.politico.com/news/stories/0608/11125.html|work=[[Politico]]|author= David Rogers|date= June 17, 2008}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|title=Dems: ICE should focus on criminals, not workers |url=http://www.newsobserver.com/politics/story/1119495.html |work=[[The News & Observer]] |author=Barbara Barrett |date=June 25, 2008 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20080715235048/http://www.newsobserver.com:80/politics/story/1119495.html |archivedate=2008-07-15 |df= }}</ref>

Price authored a provision of the [[Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997]] which made the interest on student loans tax deductible,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/bdquery/z?d105:HR02014:@@@D&summ2=m&%7cTOM:/bss/d105query.html |title=Bill Summary & Status - 105th Congress (1997 - 1998) - H.R.2014 - CRS Summary - THOMAS (Library of Congress) |publisher=Thomas.loc.gov |date= |accessdate=2012-02-10}}</ref> and legislation creating the Advanced Technological Education program at the [[National Science Foundation]], which provides grants for high-tech education in community colleges and was enacted in 1993.<ref>[http://price.house.gov/congress/legislation.shtml Price website: My Work in Congress: Legislative Accomplishments]</ref>
Price voted for the [[Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008]],<ref name="newsobserver4">

Price is the author of legislation to reform the [[public financing]] system for presidential campaigns.<ref>{{cite news|title=Point of View: Public financing's cleansing power |url=http://www.newsobserver.com/opinion/columns/story/1136500.html |work=The News & Observer |author=Chase Foster |date=July 10, 2008 }}{{dead link|date=December 2016 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref>

Price has opposed [[concentration of media ownership]]. He worked on legislative initiatives to roll back the FCC's 2003 rules <ref>{{cite web|url=http://votesmart.org/public-statement/214213/press-release-price-leads-effort-to-hold-fcc-accountable-on-media-ownership-rules#.UvEMpPuoT3A|title=Press Release - Price Leads Effort to Hold FCC Accountable on Media Ownership Rules|author= David Price|date=2006-09-21|accessdate=2014-02-04}}</ref> and co-sponsored an (unsuccessful) bill to overturn another 2008 FCC approval of media consolidation <ref>{{cite web|url=http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/query/z?c110:S.J.RES.28:|title=Bill Text Versions 110th Congress (2007-2008) S.J.RES.28|date=2008|accessdate=2014-02-04}}</ref> Price voted for the 2006 "Markey amendment" to establish [[net neutrality in the United States|network neutrality]] in the Communication Act of 1934 <ref>{{cite web|url=http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/bdquery/z?d109:HZ00987:|title=Bill Summary & Status109th Congress (2005 - 2006)H.AMDT.987 to H.R.5252 |year=2006|publisher=[[THOMAS|Thomas]]|accessdate=2013-10-23}}</ref>

In 2013, Price voted against the amendment to the [[Patriot Act]] that would have eliminated Section 215 and curtailed the National Security Agency's controversial data collection program.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.govtrack.us/congress/votes/113-2013/h412|title=H.Amdt. 413 (Amash) to H.R. 2397|year=2013|publisher=[[GovTrack]]|accessdate=2013-10-23}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Price married his wife, Lisa Kanwit, in 1968. They were longtime Democratic Party activists together,<ref name="newsobserver"/> and they have two children: Karen, a filmmaker; and Michael, a professor of Evolutionary Psychology at [[Brunel University]] in London.<ref name="about-david"/> They have two grandchildren, Charles Albert, born in 2006, and Margaret Elizabeth, born in 2009.<ref name="about-david"/> Today, Price resides in [[Chapel Hill, North Carolina|Chapel Hill]]<ref name="newsobserver"/> and is a member of the Binkley Memorial Baptist Church.

Price appeared as a contestant on the nighttime version of the [[CBS]] [[game show]] [[Password (game show)|Password]], which aired on November 28, 1963, while he was a graduate student at Yale.  His celebrity partner was Gloria Stewart, wife of [[James Stewart]], and they lost to [[James Stewart]] and his contestant partner.  Price won a consolation prize of $50.{{Citation needed|date=July 2011}}

Price received the 2011 [[John Tyler Caldwell]] Award for the Humanities from the [http://www.nchumanities.org North Carolina Humanities Council].

'''Donald W. Norcross''' (born December 13, 1958) is an American labor leader and Democratic Party politician who is currently serving as the United States Representative for [[New Jersey's 1st congressional district]]. He won the seat in 2014 following the resignation of former Rep. [[Rob Andrews]]. Prior to his service in the House, he represented the [[New Jersey Legislative Districts, 2001 redistricting#District 5|5th District]] in the [[New Jersey Senate]] from 2010-14. Norcross was sworn in as a Member of the United States House of Representatives by House Speaker [[John Boehner]] on November 12, 2014, having been elected in a special election to serve as representative of New Jersey's 1st Congressional District, and was added to the rolls of the House upon executing the oath of office.

==Personal life==
Norcross was born in 1958<ref name=Courier-Post>{{cite news|title=Another Norcross on the rise|url=http://pqasb.pqarchiver.com/courierpostonline/access/1856401991.html?FMT=ABS&amp;date=Sep+06,+2009|work=[[Courier-Post]]|first=Jane|last=Roh|date=2009-09-06}}</ref> at [[Cooper University Hospital]] in Camden, the son of George E. Norcross, Jr. He and his three brothers were raised in [[Pennsauken Township, New Jersey|Pennsauken Township]]. He graduated from [[Camden County College]] with a degree in criminal justice.<ref name=Courier-Post/>

Norcross is married to Andrea Doran, an echocardiographer. They have three children.<ref name=Courier-Post/><ref name="biodata"/>

Norcross has been active in the United Way of Camden County for close to two decades, serving as chairman from 2002 through 2004 and since as a member of the organization's Executive Board. He is currently a board member of the United Way of Greater Philadelphia and Southern New Jersey. Since 1993, he has also been a member of the Board of Trustees of the Union Organization for Social Service (UOSS), the nation's first labor agency devoted to community service. Norcross is a founding member and on the executive board of The Home Port Alliance, a coalition that brought back the retired ''[[USS New Jersey (BB-62)|USS New Jersey]]'' to serve as a monument for veterans and an attraction for the revitalization of the Camden/Philadelphia Waterfront. {{cn|date=November 2014}}

==Career==
In 1980, he served as an apprentice in the [[International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers]], eventually becoming assistant business manager of the IBEW Local 351.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://camdencountydems.com/content/norcross-why-im-running-state-assembly|title=Norcross: Why I'm running for the State Assembly|date=2009-09-02|accessdate=2009-11-03}}</ref> A former president of the Southern New Jersey Building Trades Council, Norcross served as president of the Southern New Jersey AFL-CIO Central Labor Council for 16 years.<ref name="biodata">[http://norcross.house.gov Profile], norcross.house.gov; accessed November 17, 2014.</ref>

Norcross and his running mate, Camden City Council President [[Angel Fuentes]], were elected to the Assembly in 2009 after Democratic incumbents [[Nilsa Cruz-Perez]] and [[Joseph J. Roberts]] both retired. Shortly thereafter, Norcross was appointed to the Senate seat vacated by Dana Redd who was elected as Mayor of Camden. Norcross won the Senate special election in 2010 to finish out the term, then was re-elected to the New Jersey Senate in 2011 and 2013.<ref name="phillyblogs"/><ref name="biodata"/>

===Run for Congress===
{{main|United States House of Representatives elections in New Jersey, 2014}}
On February 4, 2014, South Jersey Congressman [[Rob Andrews]] announced he would be resigning from Congress by the end of the month, and he did so on February 18.<ref name="robandrews">[http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/post-politics/wp/2014/02/04/n-j-democrat-rob-andrews-expected-to-resign-from-congress "N.J. Democrat Rob Andrews to resign from Congress"], washingtonpost.com; accessed November 17, 2014.</ref> 

Norcross announced his candidacy February 5, and within a week, he was endorsed by every New Jersey congressional Democrat, State Senate President [[Stephen Sweeney]], General Assembly Majority Leader [[Louis Greenwald]], [[Mayor of Camden, New Jersey|Mayor of Camden]] [[Dana Redd]], U.S. Senator [[Cory Booker]] and former Governor [[Jim Florio]].<ref name="phillyblogs">[http://www.philly.com/philly/blogs/capitolinq/Andrews-to-leave-Congress-in-Feb.html Rob Andrews to leave Congress], philly.com; accessed November 17, 2014.</ref>
